🌦️ PAPER 1 — Climate & Weather
01
Mid-Latitude Cyclones
Climate & Weather · Term 1
▾
What is a Mid-Latitude Cyclone?
A large-scale low-pressure system forming between 35-65 degrees latitude where cold polar air meets warm subtropical air. Forms over South Atlantic and South Indian Oceans for SA. Moves west to east.
Formation zone: South Atlantic and South Indian Oceans
Movement: West to east (westerly winds) - affects SA's southwestern Cape
Size: Can span 1,000-4,000 km across
5 Stages of Development
Stage 1 - Stationary front: Cold polar and warm subtropical air meet - no mixing yet
Stage 2 - Wave formation: Wave develops; pressure drops at crest; cyclone begins
Stage 3 - Open stage: Distinct warm front (ahead) and cold front (behind); warm sector between them
Stage 4 - Occluded stage: Faster cold front catches warm front; warm sector lifted off ground - cyclone weakens
Stage 5 - Dissolving: No temperature contrast remains; system dissipates
Weather at Fronts
WARM FRONT: Cirrus clouds to altostratus to nimbostratus. Steady prolonged rainfall (drizzle). Temperature rises gradually. Winds veer clockwise in SH. COLD FRONT: Cumulonimbus clouds. Heavy intense short-lived rain. Thunderstorms and hail. Sudden temperature drop. Strong gusty winds. Pressure rises sharply.
Impacts and Management
Positive: Rainfall replenishes Western Cape dams and agriculture (winter rainfall region)
Negative: Flooding, infrastructure damage, cold snaps harm livestock and crops
Management: SAWS forecasting, flood warning systems, disaster management plans
Synoptic map ID: V-shaped isobars; low pressure (L); cold front = filled triangles; warm front = filled semicircles
02
Tropical Cyclones
Climate & Weather · Term 1
▾
5 Formation Conditions (CAPS)
Tropical cyclones are intense rotating low-pressure systems forming over warm tropical oceans.
1. Sea surface temperature at least 26-27 degrees C to 60m depth
2. Location 5-20 degrees latitude (Coriolis effect causes rotation)
3. High humidity in lower and middle troposphere
4. Low vertical wind shear (consistent winds at all heights)
5. Pre-existing atmospheric disturbance to trigger development
Structure
Eye: Calm, clear centre; lowest pressure; light winds; warm
Eye wall: Most destructive - strongest winds, heaviest rain, cumulonimbus clouds
Spiral rainbands: Bands of cloud and rain extending outward
Rotation: Anticlockwise in Southern Hemisphere (Coriolis effect)
SA affected areas: Mozambique coast, KwaZulu-Natal, Limpopo (e.g. Cyclone Idai 2019, Freddy 2023)
Impacts and Management
Primary impacts: Storm surge, flooding, destructive winds, torrential rain, infrastructure damage
Secondary impacts: Landslides, cholera outbreaks, crop destruction, displacement
Management: Satellite tracking (EUMETSAT), early warning systems, evacuation plans, international aid
Dissipation: Cyclone weakens when it moves over land or cooler water - loses moisture and energy source
03
Anticyclones and Local Climates
Climate & Weather · Term 1
▾
Anticyclones (High Pressure)
High-pressure systems with sinking air, clear skies, and dry conditions. SA dominated by South Atlantic High and South Indian Ocean High.
Movement: Clockwise in Southern Hemisphere (air flows out from centre)
Weather: Clear skies, dry, calm winds, temperature inversions
Berg winds: Hot dry winds from interior plateau over mountains to Western Cape coast
South Indian Ocean High: Drives moisture onto SA east coast - key driver of KZN summer rainfall
Valley Climates
Anabatic wind (daytime): Sun heats slopes - warm air rises uphill - cool air flows in from valley floor
Katabatic wind (night): Slopes cool rapidly - cold dense air flows downhill into valley - frost hollow forms
Temperature inversion: Cold air trapped in valley below warmer air above - traps pollution
Urban Heat Island (UHI)
Cities are warmer than surrounding rural areas because of heat-absorbing surfaces, reduced vegetation, and human activity.
Causes: Dark surfaces (tar, brick) absorb heat; buildings trap radiation; vehicles and industry generate heat; less vegetation means less evapotranspiration
Effects: Higher night temperatures, more thunderstorms over cities, increased energy demand
Solutions: Green roofs, urban parks, reflective surfaces, urban forests, permeable paving
⛰️ PAPER 1 — Geomorphology
04
Fluvial Geomorphology (Rivers)
Geomorphology · Term 1
▾
River Processes
Erosion: Hydraulic action (force of water); Abrasion/corrasion (sand scrapes bed); Attrition (boulders collide and break); Corrosion/solution (dissolves soluble rock)
Transportation: Traction (boulders rolled along bed); Saltation (pebbles bounced); Suspension (fine silt carried); Solution (dissolved minerals)
Deposition: Occurs when velocity decreases - heavy materials deposited first, fine materials last
Upper Course Landforms
V-shaped valley: Steep sides; vertical erosion dominates; river cuts downward
Waterfalls: Hard rock overlies soft rock; soft rock erodes faster creating a step; plunge pool forms at base
Gorge: Narrow steep-sided valley formed as waterfall retreats upstream
Rapids: Alternating bands of hard and soft rock create irregular turbulent flow
Middle and Lower Course Landforms
Meanders: Erosion on outside (river cliff); deposition on inside (slip-off slope)
Oxbow lake: Meander neck erodes through; river takes shorter route; old bend cut off forming crescent-shaped lake
Flood plain: Flat land on either side built up by repeated flooding and alluvial deposits
Delta: Velocity drops at river mouth; sediment deposited; land builds outward (e.g. Nile Delta, Okavango Delta)
Levees: Raised banks built up by repeated flood deposits along river channel
Drainage Patterns
Dendritic: Tree-like; uniform rock; most common in SA
Radial: Rivers flow outward from central high point (dome or volcano)
Trellis: Right-angle pattern on folded rock with parallel main streams
Rectangular: Right-angle bends controlled by joints and faults in rock
🏘️ PAPER 2 — Settlement Geography
05
Rural Settlements
Settlement Geography · Term 2
▾
Settlement Patterns
Nucleated (clustered): Buildings grouped around a focal point (church, well, crossroads). Common where land is communally farmed.
Dispersed (scattered): Farms spread far apart on individual land. Common in commercial farming areas - Karoo, Free State.
Linear: Settlements along a line - road, river, coastline.
Location Factors
Physical: Water supply, flat fertile land, shelter from wind, safe from flooding
Human: Transport routes, proximity to markets, defence, cultural ties to land
Economic: Available resources (mining, farming, fishing), employment opportunities
Rural-Urban Migration
Push factors: Poverty, unemployment, drought, lack of services (hospitals, schools)
Pull factors: Jobs, better wages, education, healthcare, entertainment in cities
Impacts on rural areas: Ageing population, labour shortage for farming, infrastructure decay
Government response: ISRDP (Integrated Sustainable Rural Development Programme), land reform, rural infrastructure investment
06
Urban Settlements
Settlement Geography · Term 2
▾
Urban Hierarchy and Functions
Hamlet - Village - Town - City - Metropolis - Megacity (10m+ people)
Threshold population: Minimum population needed to support a particular service
Range: Maximum distance people will travel for a service
Central place theory (Christaller): Larger centres serve wider areas; hexagonal service zones
SA Urban Structure
Apartheid city model: Racially separated zones - White CBD, coloured/Indian buffer zones, Black townships far from CBD on periphery
Post-apartheid changes: Desegregation, mixed areas, gentrification, urban renewal
CBD: Highest land values, commercial core, tall buildings, maximum accessibility
Urban Challenges in SA
SA urbanisation rate: Approximately 68% urban - one of Africa's most urbanised countries
Informal settlements: No formal housing, services or sanitation; high density; prone to flooding and fire
Urban poverty: Unemployment, crime, inequality - Gini coefficient approximately 0.63
Government responses: RDP housing, BNG policy, Rea Vaya BRT (Johannesburg), urban renewal programmes
🏭 PAPER 2 — Economic Geography of SA
07
Agriculture, Mining and Industry
Economic Geography · Term 3
▾
Agriculture in SA
Commercial farming: Large-scale, profit-driven, mechanised. Intensive (market gardening, dairy) or Extensive (sheep, cattle ranching)
Subsistence farming: Small-scale, household food production, traditional methods
SA regions: Winter grain (Western Cape), summer grain (Free State), sugarcane (KZN coast), fruit (Western Cape, Limpopo), cattle (Eastern Cape, KZN)
Challenges: Drought, land reform, high input costs, load-shedding affecting irrigation
Mining in SA
Key minerals: Gold (Witwatersrand/Gauteng); Platinum (Bushveld Complex - world leader); Coal (Mpumalanga - powers Eskom); Diamonds (Kimberley); Iron ore (Northern Cape)
Environmental impacts: Acid mine drainage (AMD), land subsidence, dust pollution, spoil heaps, groundwater contamination
Social impacts: Migrant labour system disrupted families; mining towns vulnerable to closure; silicosis and mesothelioma
Management: Mining Charter (B-BBEE), NEMA compliance, rehabilitation bonds, AMD treatment plants
SA's 4 Main Industrial Regions
1. PWV/Gauteng: SA's industrial heartland - manufacturing, services, mining-linked industries
2. Durban-Pinetown-PMB (KZN): Port access, automotive (Toyota, Honda), petrochemicals, sugar processing
3. Cape Town-Bellville-Paarl (WC): Food processing, clothing, wine, tourism services
4. Gqeberha-Uitenhage (EC): Automotive hub (VW, Ford), tyres (Bridgestone), port access
Weber's Least Cost Theory: Industries locate where transport + labour + agglomeration costs are minimised
SEZs and IDZs: Tax incentives to attract investment (Coega, Richards Bay, East London)
🗺️ PAPER 1 & 2 — Mapwork and GIS
08
Mapwork Skills and GIS
Mapwork · Both Papers
▾
Topographic Map Skills
Scale 1:50,000: 1 cm = 500 m = 0.5 km. Actual distance = map distance x 50,000
Contour lines: Join points of equal altitude. Closely spaced = steep slope; widely spaced = gentle slope
Contour interval (CI): Usually 20m on SA 1:50,000 maps. Every 5th contour is an index contour (thicker, labelled)
Cross-section: Profile drawn between two points using contour heights - shows shape of land
Gradient: Vertical interval divided by horizontal equivalent. Expressed as a ratio (e.g. 1:50)
Direction and Grid References
Bearings: Measured clockwise from North 0-360 degrees. N=000, E=090, S=180, W=270
Magnetic declination: Angle between true north and magnetic north - varies by location and year
6-figure grid reference: Eastings first then northings - "along the corridor, up the stairs"
GIS (Geographic Information Systems)
Computer-based system for capturing, storing, analysing, and displaying geographic data.
Remote sensing: Data collected from a distance via satellites or aerial photography
Vector data: Points, lines, polygons (roads, rivers, farm boundaries)
Raster data: Grid of pixels (satellite images, aerial photos)
Data layers: Different information overlaid for combined analysis
Buffering: Creating a zone of specified distance around a feature
Applications: Flood mapping, urban planning, environmental monitoring, disaster management

Exam Tips
Always Attempt Mapwork (Q4)
Mapwork is predictable - the same skills appear every year. Contours, cross-sections, bearings, grid references, scale. With practice it becomes reliable free marks. Never skip Q4.
Use Geographical Terminology
Examiners reward precise language. Say "precipitation" not "water". Say "closely-spaced contours indicating steep relief" not "hilly". Technical vocabulary earns marks.
Reference the Source Material
Always quote from diagrams, maps, photos, or graphs provided. "As shown in Figure 2..." Examiners deduct marks for answers that ignore the provided sources.
SA Examples are Essential
Use SA context throughout - Cyclone Idai (Mozambique/KZN), Vaal River, Gauteng urban sprawl, Bushveld Complex platinum, Coega IDZ. Generic world examples without SA reference lose marks.
Structure 60-Mark Questions
Read ALL sub-questions first. Allocate time - roughly 1 minute per mark. Don't over-answer 2-mark sub-questions with 10 sentences. Move on once you've earned the marks.
