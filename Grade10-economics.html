Needs, Wants & Scarcity

  • Needs are essential for survival (food, water, shelter); wants are desires that improve quality of life but aren't essential.
  • Scarcity is the basic economic problem: human wants are unlimited, but resources to satisfy them are limited.

Opportunity Cost

🔑 Opportunity cost: the value of the next-best alternative given up when a choice is made — every economic decision involves a trade-off.

Factors of Production

  • Land (natural resources), labour (human effort), capital (man-made resources like machinery), and entrepreneurship (the risk-taking that combines the other three).

The Four Basic Questions

  • Every economic system must decide: what to produce, how to produce it, for whom to produce, and how to distribute income.

Types of Economic Systems

  • Traditional economy: decisions based on custom and tradition, common in subsistence communities.
  • Command (planned) economy: the government makes all major economic decisions.
  • Market economy: decisions are driven by supply, demand, and price in free markets with minimal government interference.
  • Mixed economy: combines market forces with government intervention — South Africa is a mixed economy.

The Two-Sector Model

  • Households supply factors of production (land, labour, capital, entrepreneurship) to firms and receive income (rent, wages, interest, profit) in return.
  • Firms use these factors to produce goods and services, which flow back to households in exchange for spending.

Leakages & Injections

🔑 Leakages (savings, taxes, imports) remove money from the circular flow; injections (investment, government spending, exports) add money back in.

Role of Government in the Economy

  • Government provides public goods (e.g. roads, defence) that the private sector would not efficiently provide, and regulates markets to protect consumers and workers.

Government Revenue & Expenditure

  • Government raises revenue mainly through taxation (direct taxes like income tax; indirect taxes like VAT) and spends on services such as education, health, and infrastructure.
🔑 Public goods are non-excludable and non-rivalrous — one person's use doesn't reduce availability for others (e.g. street lighting).

The Tourism Industry in South Africa

  • Tourism contributes significantly to GDP, foreign exchange earnings, and job creation in South Africa.
  • Domestic tourism involves residents travelling within their own country; international (inbound) tourism involves foreign visitors.

Benefits & Challenges

  • Benefits include employment, infrastructure development, and cultural exchange.
  • Challenges include seasonality, crime perceptions, and the environmental impact of tourism on natural attractions.

Defining Poverty

  • Absolute poverty: lacking the basic necessities needed for survival, often measured against a poverty line.
  • Relative poverty: being significantly worse off than the average standard of living in a society.

Measuring Inequality

🔑 The Gini coefficient measures income inequality on a scale from 0 (perfect equality) to 1 (perfect inequality) — South Africa has one of the highest Gini coefficients in the world.

Causes & Possible Solutions

  • Causes include unequal access to education, historical discrimination, and unemployment.
  • Solutions include social grants, improved education access, and progressive taxation.

Types of Unemployment

  • Cyclical: caused by a downturn in the business cycle. Structural: caused by a mismatch between workers' skills and available jobs. Seasonal: linked to specific times of year (e.g. farming, tourism).

Inflation

Inflation rate = ((New CPI − Old CPI) ÷ Old CPI) × 100
  • Inflation is a sustained increase in the general price level, measured in South Africa using the Consumer Price Index (CPI).
  • High inflation erodes purchasing power, meaning the same amount of money buys fewer goods and services over time.

Sustainable Development

🔑 Sustainable development meets the needs of the present without compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own needs.

Economic Activity & the Environment

  • Economic growth often comes with environmental costs, such as pollution and resource depletion — these are known as negative externalities.
  • Governments can address this through regulation, taxes on pollution, and incentives for renewable energy and conservation.

Exam Paper Guide

Understand exactly what the Grade 10 Economics papers look like and how to maximise your marks.

📄 Paper Structure

  • Section A: short answer and multiple-choice questions covering all topics.
  • Section B & C: data-response and structured questions requiring longer written answers, often with a source (graph, table, or extract) to interpret.

🔑 Command Words

  • Define/State: give a brief, precise meaning.
  • Explain: give reasons or make relationships clear, with supporting detail.
  • Discuss/Evaluate: consider different sides of an issue and reach a reasoned conclusion.
  • Illustrate: use examples or a diagram to make a point clear.

Mark Allocation Guide

Question typeWhat earns marks
Definition question (2 marks)An accurate, complete definition using correct economic terminology.
Explain question (4+ marks)A clear point followed by supporting detail or an example — one mark per developed point.
Data-response questionCorrectly reading and interpreting the given graph, table, or source before answering.
📌 Exam tip: Always use correct economic terminology (e.g. "opportunity cost", "leakages") rather than everyday language — examiners look for the specific concept being tested.
