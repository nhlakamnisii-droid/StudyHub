Study Notes

Biomolecules

  • Carbohydrates: the body's main energy source — made of carbon, hydrogen, and oxygen (e.g. glucose, starch).
  • Proteins: built from amino acids — used for growth, repair, and as enzymes.
  • Lipids (fats & oils): used for long-term energy storage and forming cell membranes.
  • Nucleic acids (DNA & RNA): carry genetic information.

Water & Inorganic Ions

🔑 Water is essential for all life processes — it acts as a solvent, a transport medium, and helps regulate body temperature.

Inorganic ions (e.g. calcium, sodium, potassium) are needed in small amounts for processes like bone formation, nerve impulses, and muscle contraction.

Cell Theory

  • All living organisms are made of cells; the cell is the basic structural and functional unit of life; all cells arise from pre-existing cells.

Prokaryotic vs Eukaryotic Cells

🔑 Prokaryotic cells (e.g. bacteria) have no nucleus or membrane-bound organelles. Eukaryotic cells (plants, animals, fungi) have a true nucleus and membrane-bound organelles.

Cell Organelles

  • Nucleus: contains DNA, controls cell activities.
  • Mitochondria: site of cellular respiration — releases energy.
  • Chloroplasts (plants only): site of photosynthesis.
  • Cell wall (plants only): provides structural support and protection.
  • Cell membrane: controls what enters and leaves the cell.

Plant vs Animal Cells

Plant cells have a cell wall, large central vacuole, and chloroplasts; animal cells lack these structures but may have small vacuoles and centrioles.

Why Mitosis Happens

Mitosis produces two genetically identical daughter cells from one parent cell — used for growth, repair, and asexual reproduction.

The Phases of Mitosis

  1. Prophase: chromosomes condense and become visible; the nuclear membrane breaks down.
  2. Metaphase: chromosomes line up along the middle (equator) of the cell.
  3. Anaphase: sister chromatids are pulled apart to opposite poles.
  4. Telophase: nuclear membranes reform around each set of chromosomes.
🔑 Cytokinesis: the division of the cytoplasm, which follows telophase and produces two separate daughter cells.

Levels of Organisation

Cell → Tissue → Organ → System → Organism

Plant Tissues

  • Meristematic tissue: actively dividing cells found at root and shoot tips, responsible for growth.
  • Permanent tissue: mature tissue that has stopped dividing (e.g. epidermis, xylem, phloem).

Animal Tissues

  • Epithelial tissue: covers body surfaces and lines cavities.
  • Connective tissue: supports and binds other tissues (e.g. bone, blood).
  • Muscle tissue: contracts to produce movement.
  • Nervous tissue: transmits electrical impulses.

Why We Classify Organisms

Classification groups organisms by shared characteristics, making it easier to identify, study, and communicate about the huge diversity of life.

The Five Kingdoms

  • Monera: prokaryotic, single-celled organisms (bacteria).
  • Protista: simple eukaryotic organisms, mostly single-celled (e.g. amoeba, algae).
  • Fungi: eukaryotic, absorb nutrients from their surroundings (e.g. mushrooms, yeast).
  • Plantae: multicellular, photosynthetic organisms.
  • Animalia: multicellular organisms that consume food for energy.
🔑 Binomial nomenclature: the system of naming organisms with two names — genus and species (e.g. Homo sapiens) — always italicised or underlined.

Levels of the Environment

Biosphere → Biome → Ecosystem → Community → Population → Organism

Abiotic & Biotic Factors

  • Abiotic factors: non-living components of an ecosystem — e.g. temperature, water, sunlight, soil.
  • Biotic factors: living components — e.g. plants, animals, microorganisms.
🔑 Ecosystem: all the living organisms in an area, interacting with each other and with their non-living environment.

Food Chains & Food Webs

  • A food chain shows a single path of energy transfer: Producer → Primary consumer → Secondary consumer → Tertiary consumer.
  • A food web shows the interconnected feeding relationships between many food chains in an ecosystem.
🔑 Energy loss: only about 10% of energy is passed from one trophic level to the next — the rest is lost as heat, so energy decreases up the food chain.

Nutrient Cycles

  • Carbon cycle: carbon moves between the atmosphere, living organisms, and the ground through photosynthesis, respiration, and decomposition.
  • Nitrogen cycle: nitrogen is converted between forms (e.g. by nitrogen-fixing bacteria) so it can be used by plants and animals.

Pollution

  • Air pollution: from vehicle emissions and industry, contributing to respiratory illness and acid rain.
  • Water pollution: from sewage, chemicals, and plastics, harming aquatic ecosystems.

Global Warming & Climate Change

🔑 Greenhouse effect: gases like carbon dioxide trap heat in the atmosphere — human activity has increased these gases, intensifying the natural greenhouse effect and warming the planet.

Conservation

Protecting biodiversity through actions like establishing nature reserves, reducing deforestation, and promoting sustainable resource use helps maintain healthy ecosystems for the future.

Exam Paper Guide

Understand exactly what the Grade 10 Life Sciences papers look like and how to maximise your marks.

📄 Paper Overview

  • Paper 1: typically covers cellular & molecular content — chemistry of life, cells, cell division, and tissues.
  • Paper 2: typically covers environmental content — biodiversity & classification, ecosystems, energy flow, and human impact.

🔑 Levels of Questions

  • Level 1 (Recall): stating a definition or labelling a diagram.
  • Level 2 (Understanding): explaining a process or comparing two structures.
  • Level 3 (Application): applying knowledge to a new scenario or interpreting data.
  • Level 4 (Evaluation): analysing an investigation or evaluating an environmental issue.

Mark Allocation Guide

Question typeWhat earns marks
Definition questionThe exact, complete biological definition — missing one key term can lose the mark.
Labelling a diagramCorrectly identifying the structure with the exact term used in the curriculum.
Describe/Explain questionMarks are awarded per correct, distinct point made — write in full, precise biological sentences.
📌 Exam tip: Use precise biological terminology — "it changes" or "it moves" is too vague to earn marks; name the exact process or structure.

Diagram & Process-Writing Guide

Life Sciences exams reward clear diagrams and well-structured process explanations — here's how to do both for full marks.

1

Drawing Biological Diagrams

  • Use a sharp pencil, single continuous lines — never sketchy or shaded lines.
  • Draw large enough to show detail clearly (at least half a page for cell/organ diagrams).
  • Label lines must be straight, drawn with a ruler, and must not cross each other.
  • Always include a title and, where relevant, the magnification or scale.
2

Answering Process Questions (e.g. "Describe mitosis")

Write process answers as a numbered, chronological sequence — examiners award one mark per correct, ordered step.

Step 1: [first stage of the process]
Step 2: [next stage, in the correct order]
Step 3: [continue until the process is complete]
3

Interpreting Graphs & Data

  • Always state the trend first (increases/decreases/stays constant), then quote actual values from the axes as evidence.
  • Link the trend to the underlying biology — don't just describe the shape of the graph.
As [independent variable] increases, [dependent variable] [increases/decreases] from [value] to [value]. This is because [biological reason].
4

Investigations & Variables

  • Independent variable: the factor the experimenter deliberately changes.
  • Dependent variable: the factor that is measured/observed as a result.
  • Controlled variables: factors kept constant to ensure a fair test.
  • Always include a control group/setup to compare results against.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

❌ Messy or Unlabelled Diagrams

Diagrams without a title, labels, or with crossed label lines lose easy marks even if the drawing itself is accurate.

❌ Vague Language

"The cell does its job" isn't an answer — use precise biological terminology every time.

❌ Skipping Steps in a Process

Leaving out one phase of mitosis, or one stage of a nutrient cycle, costs a mark per missing step.

❌ Describing a Graph Without Explaining It

Stating "the line goes up" without linking it to the underlying biology only earns partial marks.

🔬 Practice tip: Redraw and label the key diagrams (a plant/animal cell, the phases of mitosis, a food web) from memory each week until you can do it without checking a textbook.
