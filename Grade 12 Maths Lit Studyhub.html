💰 Finance
01
Interest (Simple & Compound)
Finance · Paper 1
▾
Simple Interest
Interest calculated ONLY on the original principal — does not grow over time.
A = P(1 + i × n)
OR
I = P × i × n

P = principal (starting amount)
i = interest rate (as decimal, e.g. 8% = 0.08)
n = number of years
Worked Example
Sipho invests R8,000 at 7% simple interest for 3 years.
I = 8,000 × 0.07 × 3 = R1,680
A = 8,000 + 1,680 = R9,680
Compound Interest
Interest calculated on the principal AND on previously earned interest. Grows faster than simple interest.
A = P(1 + i)ⁿ

P = principal
i = interest rate per period (as decimal)
n = number of periods
Worked Example
Zanele invests R10,000 at 9% compound interest for 4 years.
A = 10,000 × (1 + 0.09)⁴
A = 10,000 × (1.09)⁴ = 10,000 × 1.4116
A = R14,115.82
Interest earned = 14,115.82 − 10,000 = R4,115.82
Hire Purchase (HP)
Buying goods on credit by paying a deposit and monthly instalments. HP always uses SIMPLE interest calculated on the FULL original price.
Deposit = % × cash price (must be paid upfront)
Amount financed = cash price − deposit
Interest = amount financed × rate × years (simple interest)
Total HP cost = deposit + amount financed + total interest
Monthly instalment = (amount financed + interest) ÷ number of months
Worked Example
TV costs R12,000. Deposit: 10%. HP rate: 15% p.a. over 2 years.
Deposit = 0.10 × 12,000 = R1,200
Amount financed = 12,000 − 1,200 = R10,800
Interest = 10,800 × 0.15 × 2 = R3,240
Monthly instalment = (10,800 + 3,240) ÷ 24 = R585/month
02
Tax, Income & Financial Documents
Finance · Paper 1 & 2
▾
Income Tax (PAYE)
Tax deducted from salaries/wages before payment — Pay As You Earn. Calculated using SARS tax tables.
Gross income = total earnings before any deductions
Taxable income = gross income − allowable deductions (e.g. pension, medical aid)
Tax payable = tax from table − primary rebate (R17,235 for under 65s in 2024/25)
Net pay (take-home) = gross income − PAYE − UIF − medical aid − pension
UIF contribution = 1% of gross salary (employee) + 1% (employer) — max R177.12/month
VAT (Value Added Tax)
A consumption tax added to goods and services in SA. Current VAT rate = 15%.
Price including VAT = Price excluding VAT × 1.15
Price excluding VAT = Price including VAT ÷ 1.15
VAT amount = Price incl. VAT × (15/115) = Price incl. × 0.1304
Zero-rated items: basic foods — brown bread, maize meal, milk, rice, vegetables, eggs, fruit → VAT = 0%
Exempt items: educational fees, residential rent, public transport → no VAT charged
Worked Example
A jacket costs R460 including VAT. What is the VAT amount and price excluding VAT?
Price excl. VAT = 460 ÷ 1.15 = R400
VAT = 460 − 400 = R60
Exchange Rates
The rate at which one currency converts to another.
Foreign currency = Rands ÷ Exchange rate
Rands = Foreign currency × Exchange rate
Worked Example
R1 = 0.053 USD. Convert R5,000 to USD:
USD = 5,000 × 0.053 = $265
Convert $150 to Rands (rate: $1 = R18.87):
Rands = 150 × 18.87 = R2,830.50
Financial Documents
Payslip: shows gross income, deductions (PAYE, UIF, medical aid, pension), and net pay
Invoice: itemised bill from supplier requesting payment
Receipt: proof that payment was made
Budget: planned income vs planned expenditure — surplus (income > expense) or deficit
Bank statement: record of deposits, withdrawals, and balance — debit (money out), credit (money in)
Tariff table: table showing cost based on usage (e.g. electricity, water — tiered rates)
📐 Measurement
03
Perimeter, Area & Volume
Measurement · Paper 1 & 2
▾
Perimeter
Rectangle: P = 2(l + w)
Square: P = 4s
Circle (Circumference): C = 2πr = πd (π ≈ 3.142)
Triangle: P = a + b + c (sum of all sides)
Area
Rectangle: A = l × w
Square: A = s²
Triangle: A = ½ × b × h
Circle: A = π × r²
Trapezium: A = ½(a + b) × h
Worked Example
A circular garden has a diameter of 14 m. Find the area.
r = 14 ÷ 2 = 7 m
A = π × 7² = 3.142 × 49 = 153.96 m²
Volume
Rectangular prism: V = l × w × h
Cylinder: V = π × r² × h
Triangular prism: V = (½ × b × h) × length

1 litre = 1,000 cm³
1 m³ = 1,000 litres
Worked Example
A cylindrical tank has radius 0.5 m and height 1.2 m. Find volume in litres.
V = π × (0.5)² × 1.2 = 3.142 × 0.25 × 1.2 = 0.9426 m³
= 0.9426 × 1,000 = 942.6 litres
Surface Area
Rectangular prism: SA = 2(lw + lh + wh)
Cylinder: SA = 2πr² + 2πrh
Cube: SA = 6s²
Tip: Surface area counts the area of ALL faces/surfaces of a 3D shape
Open containers (no lid): subtract ONE circular face from cylinder formula
Unit Conversions
Length: 1 km = 1,000 m = 100,000 cm = 1,000,000 mm
Mass: 1 kg = 1,000 g | 1 tonne = 1,000 kg
Capacity: 1 L = 1,000 mL | 1 kL = 1,000 L
Area: 1 m² = 10,000 cm² | 1 hectare = 10,000 m²
Time: 1 hr = 60 min | 1 min = 60 sec
🗺️ Maps, Plans & Scale
04
Scale, Maps & Building Plans
Maps & Plans · Paper 2
▾
Scale
Scale shows the ratio between a measurement on a map/plan and the actual measurement in real life.
Actual distance = Map distance × Scale denominator
Map distance = Actual distance ÷ Scale denominator

Example: Scale 1:50 means 1 cm on map = 50 cm in reality
Worked Example
A floor plan uses scale 1:100. A room measures 4.5 cm on the plan.
Actual length = 4.5 × 100 = 450 cm = 4.5 m
Maps & Navigation
Compass directions: N, S, E, W, NE, NW, SE, SW
Grid references: column letter first, then row number (e.g. B3)
Strip maps: route maps showing roads and distances between points
Speed, Distance, Time:
Distance = Speed × Time
Speed = Distance ÷ Time
Time = Distance ÷ Speed

Convert km/h to m/s: ÷ 3.6
Convert m/s to km/h: × 3.6
Worked Example
A car travels 240 km at 80 km/h. How long does the trip take?
Time = 240 ÷ 80 = 3 hours
Reading Building Plans
Floor plan: top-view (bird's-eye) diagram showing room layout and dimensions
Elevation: side or front view of a building
Read dimensions carefully — usually given in mm on professional plans
Calculate area by multiplying length × width (convert mm to m first: ÷ 1,000)
Calculate tiles needed: Area ÷ tile size (add 10% for wastage)
📊 Data Handling & Probability
05
Data Handling (Statistics)
Data Handling · Paper 2
▾
Measures of Central Tendency
Mean = Sum of all values ÷ Number of values
Median = Middle value when data is ordered
Mode = Value that appears most often
Range = Maximum − Minimum
Worked Example — Data set: 4, 7, 3, 9, 7, 2, 8
Ordered: 2, 3, 4, 7, 7, 8, 9
Mean = (2+3+4+7+7+8+9) ÷ 7 = 40 ÷ 7 = 5.71
Median = 7 (middle value, 4th of 7)
Mode = 7 (appears twice)
Range = 9 − 2 = 7
Five-Number Summary & Box-and-Whisker
Minimum: lowest value
Q1 (Lower Quartile): median of the lower half
Q2 (Median): middle value of full data set
Q3 (Upper Quartile): median of the upper half
Maximum: highest value
IQR (Interquartile Range): Q3 − Q1 (shows spread of middle 50% of data)
Graphs & Charts
Bar graph: comparing categories using bars
Histogram: shows frequency distribution of continuous data — bars touch each other
Pie chart: shows proportions as slices — each slice = (frequency ÷ total) × 360°
Line graph: shows trends over time
Scatter plot: shows relationship between two variables; trendline shows positive/negative/no correlation
06
Probability
Data Handling · Paper 2
▾
Basic Probability
Probability measures how likely an event is to happen. Always between 0 (impossible) and 1 (certain).
P(event) = Number of favourable outcomes ÷ Total possible outcomes

P(event) + P(not event) = 1
P(not A) = 1 − P(A)
Worked Example
A bag has 4 red, 3 blue, and 5 green balls. Probability of picking red?
Total = 4 + 3 + 5 = 12
P(red) = 4 ÷ 12 = 1/3 ≈ 0.33 = 33.3%
Tree Diagrams & Two-Way Tables
Tree diagram: shows all possible outcomes of sequential events — multiply along branches for combined probability
Two-way table: organises outcomes of two events in rows and columns to find probabilities
With replacement: probabilities stay the same for each event
Without replacement: total decreases after each draw — probabilities change
07
Patterns, Graphs & Number Systems
Numbers · Paper 1
▾
Number Types & Percentages
% of a value: (percentage ÷ 100) × value
% change: ((new − old) ÷ old) × 100
% increase/decrease: New = Old × (1 ± % ÷ 100)
Ratio: simplify by dividing both by HCF. e.g. 12:18 = 2:3
Proportion: if A:B = C:D then A×D = B×C (cross-multiply)
Linear Patterns & Graphs
A linear pattern has a constant difference between consecutive terms. It graphs as a straight line.
y = mx + c
m = gradient (rate of change) = (y₂-y₁)÷(x₂-x₁)
c = y-intercept (value when x = 0)
Reading graphs: find value on x-axis → go up to line → read off y-axis (or vice versa)
Break-even: where two lines intersect — costs = income
Time & Timetables
12-hour clock: am (midnight–noon) and pm (noon–midnight)
24-hour clock: add 12 to any pm hour: 3:00 pm = 15:00
Duration: end time − start time. If crossing midnight, add 24 hours to end time first.
Bus/train timetables: read departure and arrival columns; calculate journey time and connections

Exam Tips
Show ALL Working
Even if your final answer is wrong, you earn method marks for correct working. Write each step clearly. Never just write an answer with no working — you risk zero for that question.
Units Always
Always include units in your answers: cm, m², litres, R, %, km/h. Leaving out units often costs a mark. Check what units the question asks for before writing your final answer.
Use the Annexure (Paper 2)
The annexure has maps, plans, and graphs. Read it carefully before attempting questions — highlight key measurements. Many students lose marks by misreading the scale or ignoring information in the annexure.
Rounding Rules
For money: always round to 2 decimal places (cents). For percentages: round to 1 or 2 decimal places unless told otherwise. For tiles/people/items: always round UP (you can't buy half a tile). For time: express as hours and minutes, not decimals.
Calculator Tips
Use your calculator for every calculation — don't try to do it mentally. For compound interest: use the power function (x^n or yˣ). Double-check by estimating first. Make sure your calculator is not in scientific notation mode.
Context is King
Maths Lit always uses real-life contexts. Read the scenario carefully before calculating. The context tells you which formula to use and what units are relevant. Re-read the question after calculating to make sure your answer makes sense in real life.
