Term 1 · Macroeconomics
01
Circular Flow
Term 1 · Macroeconomics
▾
Key Concepts
The circular flow model shows how money, goods, and services move between households, businesses, government, and the foreign sector in an open economy.
Open economy = includes foreign sector (exports/imports)
Leakages (withdrawals) = savings (S), taxes (T), imports (M) — money leaving the flow
Injections = investment (I), government spending (G), exports (X) — money entering the flow
Equilibrium: S + T + M = I + G + X
The Multiplier
The multiplier shows how an initial injection leads to a larger total increase in national income.
Multiplier (k) = 1 ÷ (1 - MPC) = 1 ÷ MPS
MPC = Marginal Propensity to Consume (fraction spent)
MPS = Marginal Propensity to Save (fraction saved)
If MPS = 0.25, then k = 1 ÷ 0.25 = 4 → R100 injection = R400 total income increase
National Account Aggregates
GDP (P) = Production approach: sum of all value added
GDP (E) = Expenditure: C + I + G + (X − M)
GDP (I) = Income: wages + rent + interest + profit
GNI = GDP + net factor income from abroad
02
Business Cycles
Term 1 · Macroeconomics
▾
Phases of the Business Cycle
Business cycles are recurring fluctuations in economic activity over time — expansions followed by contractions.
Prosperity (Peak) — highest point; high employment, output, prices
Recession (Downswing) — GDP falls for 2+ consecutive quarters
Depression (Trough) — lowest point; high unemployment, low output
Recovery (Upswing) — GDP starts rising again
Causes (Exogenous vs Endogenous)
Exogenous: natural disasters, wars, oil price shocks, global pandemics
Endogenous: changes in investment, consumer confidence, monetary policy
Leading indicators: predict future cycles (e.g. new car sales, share prices)
Lagging indicators: confirm past trends (e.g. unemployment rate, bank interest rates)
Coincident indicators: move with the cycle (e.g. GDP, retail sales)
Government Policy Response
Recession: government uses expansionary fiscal/monetary policy to boost demand
Boom: contractionary policy to prevent overheating and inflation
New Economic Paradigm: aims to smooth out cycles via stable inflation targeting and fiscal discipline
03
Public Sector
Term 1 · Macroeconomics
▾
Role of the Public Sector
The government plays an allocative, distributive, and stabilisation role in the economy.
Fiscal policy = government use of taxation and spending to influence the economy
Expansionary fiscal policy: increase spending / cut taxes → stimulate growth
Contractionary fiscal policy: cut spending / raise taxes → reduce inflation
Budget surplus: revenue > expenditure
Budget deficit: expenditure > revenue (SA's common situation)
The Laffer Curve
Shows relationship between tax rates and tax revenue. At 0% and 100% tax rates, revenue = 0. Maximum revenue is earned at an optimal middle rate.
Too high tax rates → people evade tax or work less → revenue falls
SA concern: bracket creep and high personal income tax burden on middle class
Public Sector Failures
Corruption and mismanagement of SOEs (e.g. Eskom, SAA)
Inefficient delivery of public goods
Short-term political decision-making vs long-term economic needs
04
Foreign Exchange Markets
Term 1 · Globalisation
▾
Exchange Rates
The exchange rate is the price of one currency in terms of another.
Appreciation: rand buys more foreign currency → exports more expensive, imports cheaper
Depreciation: rand buys less → exports cheaper, imports more expensive → inflationary
Factors affecting rand: inflation, interest rates, political stability, trade balance, commodity prices
Balance of Payments (BOP)
Current account: trade in goods & services + income + transfers
Capital account: transfer of assets (e.g. debt forgiveness)
Financial account: investment flows (FDI, portfolio investment)
BOP surplus: more inflows → rand appreciates
BOP deficit: more outflows → rand depreciates
Term 2 · Microeconomics
05
Perfect & Imperfect Markets
Term 2 · Microeconomics
▾
Perfect Competition
Many buyers and sellers, homogeneous products, free entry/exit, perfect information. No single firm can influence price.
Price taker — firms accept market price (P = MR)
Profit maximised where MC = MR
Long run: economic profit = 0 (normal profit only)
Imperfect Markets
Monopoly: single seller, unique product, high barriers to entry. Price maker. P > MC. Examples: Eskom (electricity), SANRAL (toll roads)
Oligopoly: few large firms, interdependent. May collude (cartel). Examples: SA banking (Big 5), cement industry
Monopolistic competition: many firms, differentiated products, some price control. Examples: restaurants, hair salons
Market Failures
When the free market fails to allocate resources efficiently.
Externalities: negative (pollution) or positive (education spillovers)
Public goods: non-excludable, non-rival (e.g. street lights, national defence)
Information asymmetry: one party has more info than another
Government response: taxes on negative externalities, subsidies for positive ones
06
Protectionism & Free Trade
Term 2 · Globalisation
▾
Protectionism vs Free Trade
Protectionism: tariffs, quotas, subsidies to shield local industries from foreign competition
Arguments for: protect infant industries, preserve jobs, national security, revenue for government
Free trade: no restrictions on imports/exports between countries
Arguments for: lower prices, greater variety, efficiency, economic growth
SA approach: mix of both — SADC trade agreements + some import tariffs on sensitive industries
Term 3 · Economic Pursuits & Contemporary Issues
07
Economic Growth & Development
Term 3 · Economic Pursuits
▾
Growth vs Development
Economic growth = increase in real GDP over time.
Economic development = improvement in living standards, reduction of poverty, human development.
Demand-side approach: boost consumer spending and government expenditure (Keynesian)
Supply-side approach: reduce taxes, deregulate, improve infrastructure to increase productive capacity
North/South divide: developed (North) vs developing (South) countries. SA is part of the Global South.
Performance Indicators
Economic: GDP, inflation rate, unemployment rate, interest rates, balance of trade
Social: HDI (Human Development Index), Gini coefficient, literacy rate, life expectancy
Gini coefficient: measures income inequality. 0 = perfect equality, 1 = perfect inequality. SA has one of the world's highest (~0.63)
Industrial Development (SA)
IPAP (Industrial Policy Action Plan) — promotes manufacturing
SEZ (Special Economic Zones) — designated areas with tax incentives to attract investment
SMME support — via SEDA, NEF, IDC
08
Inflation, Tourism & Environment
Term 3 · Contemporary Issues
▾
Inflation
A sustained general rise in the price level, measured by the Consumer Price Index (CPI).
Demand-pull: too much money chasing too few goods
Cost-push: rising production costs push prices up (e.g. oil, wages, electricity)
Imported inflation: weak rand makes imports more expensive
SA target: SARB targets 3%–6% inflation band
Policy response: SARB raises repo rate → borrowing more expensive → spending falls → inflation cools
Tourism
Tourism contributes ~3% of SA GDP and creates ~1.5 million jobs
Benefits: foreign exchange earnings, employment, infrastructure development, cultural exchange
SA advantages: Big 5, natural beauty, cultural heritage, weak rand (affordable for foreigners)
Challenges: crime, poor infrastructure, load-shedding, visa restrictions
Environmental Sustainability
Rio Summit (1992): introduced concept of sustainable development
Johannesburg Summit (2002): focused on implementing Rio commitments in SA context
Sustainable development: meeting present needs without compromising future generations
SA issues: coal dependence, water scarcity, deforestation, e-waste

Exam Tips
Section C Essay Format
Always include: Introduction, main body with headings, conclusion. Use subheadings — they earn format marks. Write at least 15 factual points in the body (each = 2 marks). Aim for 30+ marks in the body.
Data Response (Section B)
Read the extract/graph carefully before answering. Quote from the source when it helps. For "explain" questions, always give 2 marks worth — define + expand or cause + effect.
Time Management
Section A: 20 min. Section B: 45 min (2 questions × ~22 min). Section C: 30 min. Leave 5 min to check. Don't skip Section A — it's guaranteed marks.
South African Context
Examiners love SA examples. Always link theory to SA reality — Eskom (monopoly), SARB (monetary policy), NDP (development), load-shedding (business cycles), Gini coefficient (inequality).
