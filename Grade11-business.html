Macro Environment

The macro environment is the external environment a business cannot control — it can only monitor and react to it.

  • PESTEL factors: Political, Economic, Social, Technological, Environmental (ecological), Legal.
  • Political: government stability, trade policy, BEE legislation, labour laws.
  • Economic: inflation, interest rates, exchange rates, unemployment.
  • Social: population growth, culture, consumer attitudes, lifestyle trends.
  • Technological: automation, e-commerce, AI, rate of innovation.
  • Environmental/ecological: climate change, pollution, sustainability pressure.
  • Legal: consumer protection, labour relations, competition law.
🔑 Macro environment: the external environment a business has no control over — only the ability to react and adapt.

Market Environment

  • Customers — needs, expectations, buying power.
  • Competitors — direct and indirect rivals for the same customers.
  • Suppliers — provide raw materials, stock, services.
  • Intermediaries — wholesalers, retailers, distributors.
  • The public — media, pressure groups, local community.
🔑 Market environment: the environment in which the business operates directly — the business has limited control here through strategy.

Micro Environment

  • Vision & mission statements.
  • Business resources: human, physical, financial, intellectual.
  • Internal policies, structure, and culture.
🔑 Micro environment: the internal environment — the business has full control here.

Environmental Scanning & Strategy

  • Environmental scanning: continuously monitoring all three environments to spot threats and opportunities early.
  • Businesses respond to PESTEL challenges through diversification, lobbying, CSR programmes, and adopting new technology.
  • SWOT analysis: Strengths & Weaknesses (internal/micro) vs Opportunities & Threats (external/macro & market).

Forms of Ownership

  • Sole trader: one owner, unlimited liability, no separate legal personality, cheap and easy to start.
  • Partnership: 2–20 partners, usually unlimited (joint) liability, based on a partnership agreement.
  • Private company (Pty Ltd): 1+ shareholders, limited liability, separate legal personality, shares not offered to the public.
  • Public company (Ltd): shares traded on the stock exchange (e.g. JSE), limited liability, strict reporting requirements.
  • Personal liability company (Inc): for professionals (e.g. attorneys); directors jointly liable for company debts.
  • Cooperative: owned and run by members for mutual benefit, one member one vote regardless of shares held.
  • Non-profit company (NPC): formed for a public benefit purpose, not shareholder profit.
  • State-owned enterprise (SOE): owned and controlled by government, e.g. Eskom, Transnet.
  • Franchise: the franchisee buys the right to use an established business's name, systems and branding.

Sectors of the Economy

  • Primary extraction of raw materials — farming, mining, fishing.
  • Secondary manufacturing & processing raw materials into products.
  • Tertiary providing services — retail, banking, transport, tourism.
  • Quaternary knowledge-based services — research, IT, consulting.

Starting a Venture

🔑 Feasibility study: investigates whether a business idea is realistic and worth pursuing — done before committing resources.
🔑 Business plan: a detailed written document describing how the business will be set up, financed, marketed and operated — used to secure funding.
  • Key entrepreneurial qualities: risk-taking, innovation, perseverance, self-discipline, opportunity-seeking.

Creative Thinking & Problem Solving

  • Techniques: brainstorming, mind mapping, Six Thinking Hats, SCAMPER.
  • Problem-solving steps: identify the problem → gather information → generate options → evaluate & choose → implement → review.

Leadership Styles

  • Autocratic: leader decides alone, little team input.
  • Democratic: leader consults the team before deciding.
  • Laissez-faire: leader gives the team freedom to decide.
  • Charismatic: leads through personal appeal and inspiration.
  • Transactional: leads through rewards and clear structure.
  • Transformational: leads by inspiring change and a shared vision.

Team Performance & Motivation

  • Tuckman's stages: Forming → Storming → Norming → Performing → Adjourning.
  • Maslow's hierarchy of needs: Physiological → Safety → Social → Esteem → Self-actualisation; people are motivated by the next unmet level.
  • Herzberg's two-factor theory: hygiene factors (salary, conditions) prevent dissatisfaction; motivators (recognition, growth) drive satisfaction.

Self-Management

🔑 SMART goals: Specific, Measurable, Achievable, Realistic, Time-bound.
  • Time management, stress management, and self-discipline are core to sustaining business roles.

Business Functions Overview

  • General management: planning, organising, leading, controlling the whole business.
  • Human resources: recruitment, training, performance management, labour relations.
  • Public relations: managing the business's image and relationship with stakeholders.
  • Marketing: identifying and satisfying customer needs profitably (the marketing mix: Product, Price, Place, Promotion).
  • Production/operations: converting inputs into goods or services.
  • Financial: managing money — budgeting, record-keeping, financial reporting.
  • Administration: managing information, records and office systems.
  • Purchasing: sourcing and buying the resources the business needs.

Quality of Performance

🔑 Total Quality Management (TQM): a management approach where every employee continuously works to improve quality across all business functions.
  • Quality indicators: reliability, consistency, customer satisfaction, conformance to standards.
  • TQM principles: customer focus, continuous improvement (Kaizen), employee involvement, process-centred thinking.

Exam Paper Guide

Understand exactly what the Business Studies paper looks like and how to maximise your marks.

📄 Paper Overview

  • Duration: 3 hours
  • Total marks: 300
  • Section A: Compulsory short-answer/objective questions (~80 marks)
  • Section B: Structured, case-study based questions across all topics (~120 marks)
  • Section C: Choose 2 of 4 essay-type questions (2 × 50 = 100 marks)

🔑 Command Words

  • Name/State/List: 1 mark per correct fact — no explanation needed.
  • Explain/Discuss: 2 marks per point — fact + motivation or example.
  • Analyse/Evaluate: requires judgement, comparison, or applying theory to a scenario.
  • Recommend/Suggest: give a practical solution linked to the case study.

Mark Allocation Guide

Question typeMarks per pointWhat's expected
Define/Name/List1 markA single correct fact or term, no elaboration.
Explain/Discuss2 marksA fact plus a reason, example, or short motivation.
Case-study application2 marksA theory point applied specifically to the scenario given.
Essay (Section C)50 marksStructured, well-argued response covering content, layout, and a conclusion.
📌 Exam tip: Always check the mark allocation next to a question — it tells you exactly how many points the examiner expects.

Case Study Answering Technique

Business Studies questions are almost always built around a short business scenario — here's how to answer them for full marks.

1

Read the Case Study Twice

First read for the overall story — what business is this, what's the situation? Second read, underline names, numbers, and any specific problem mentioned.

2

Identify the Command Word & Marks

Check whether the question asks you to name, explain, analyse, or recommend — and how many marks it's worth. This tells you how much detail to give.

3

Apply Theory to the Scenario — Don't Write Generically

A generic textbook answer earns few marks. You must link your point directly to the business in the case study.

[Business in the case study] shows [theory/concept] because [specific evidence from the case study]. This means [effect/consequence for the business].
4

Use Correct Business Terminology

Examiners look for the exact CAPS terms — e.g. "macro environment" not "outside stuff," "liquidity" not "having cash."

Marking Rubric — What the Examiner Looks For

CriteriaWhat earns the mark
RelevanceAnswer is directly linked to the case study, not generic theory.
TerminologyCorrect CAPS business vocabulary is used.
ApplicationTheory is applied to the specific scenario given, with evidence quoted from the text.
StructureAnswer matches the mark allocation — neither too short nor padded.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

❌ Generic Theory Only

Don't just recite a definition. Always connect it back to the business named in the case study.

❌ Ignoring Mark Allocation

A 2-mark question needs two points — one-word answers or single facts lose marks.

❌ Wrong Terminology

"The business has no money" should read "the business has a liquidity problem."

❌ Not Reading Carefully

Many learners miss key details (numbers, names, dates) given in the case study that the question depends on.

🧩 Practice tip: After studying a topic, write your own 2-line mini case study and answer one question on it — this builds the "apply, don't recite" habit examiners reward.
