Study Notes

Exponents & Surds

  • Laws of exponents: am·an = am+n; am÷an = am−n; (am)n = amn; a0 = 1 (a≠0); a−n = 1/aⁿ.
  • Surds are simplified by extracting perfect square (or cube) factors, e.g. √50 = 5√2.

Factorisation

  • Common factor: take out the highest common factor first, always.
  • Difference of two squares: a² − b² = (a−b)(a+b).
  • Trinomials: find two numbers that multiply to give "c" and add to give "b" in ax²+bx+c.
  • Sum/difference of cubes: a³+b³ = (a+b)(a²−ab+b²); a³−b³ = (a−b)(a²+ab+b²).
  • Grouping: used for four-term expressions with no obvious common factor.

Solving Equations

🔑 Quadratic formula: used when a quadratic cannot be factorised easily — x = (−b ± √(b²−4ac)) / 2a.
  • Nature of roots (discriminant Δ = b²−4ac): Δ>0 gives two distinct real roots; Δ=0 gives one repeated real root; Δ<0 gives no real roots.
  • If Δ is a perfect square and Δ>0, the roots are rational; otherwise irrational.
  • Simultaneous equations (linear & quadratic): make one variable the subject in the linear equation, substitute into the quadratic, then solve.
  • Quadratic inequalities: factorise, find critical values, then use a number line or sketch to determine the solution region.

Arithmetic Sequences

  • A sequence with a constant common difference (d) between consecutive terms.
  • General term: Tn = a + (n−1)d, where a is the first term.

Quadratic Sequences

🔑 Second difference: for a quadratic sequence, the second differences between terms are constant (unlike arithmetic sequences, where the first differences are constant).
  • General term takes the form Tn = an² + bn + c.
  • To find a, b, c: use the constant second difference (= 2a), then work backwards using the first differences and first term.

Working with Patterns

  • Always write out the first few terms and the differences between them before attempting to find a formula.
  • Check your general term formula by substituting n=1, n=2, n=3 back in — it must reproduce the original sequence.

The Parabola

y = ax² + bx + c (or y = a(x−p)² + q)
  • If a>0 the parabola opens upward (∪-shape, minimum turning point); if a<0 it opens downward (∩-shape, maximum turning point).
  • Turning point at (p,q); axis of symmetry x = p; y-intercept at (0,c).

The Hyperbola

y = a/(x−p) + q
  • Vertical asymptote at x = p; horizontal asymptote at y = q.
  • If a>0, branches lie in quadrants where (x−p) and (y−q) have the same sign; if a<0, opposite sign.

The Exponential Function

y = a·bx + q (b > 0, b ≠ 1)
  • Horizontal asymptote at y = q.
  • If b>1 the function increases; if 0<b<1 it decreases.

Reading & Sketching Graphs

  • Always find and label: intercepts with the axes, turning point(s), and asymptotes (if any).
  • To find the equation of a graph from a sketch, substitute known points/intercepts into the general form and solve for the unknown constants.

Simple vs Compound Growth

Simple growth: A = P(1 + i·n)
Compound growth: A = P(1 + i)ⁿ
  • Simple growth/decay is calculated only on the original principal; compound growth/decay is calculated on the accumulated amount each period, so it grows (or shrinks) faster.

Decay

Simple decay: A = P(1 − i·n)
Compound decay: A = P(1 − i)ⁿ
🔑 Depreciation: the decrease in value of an asset over time — modelled using either simple (straight-line) or compound (reducing-balance) decay.

Nominal vs Effective Interest Rates

  • Nominal rate: the stated annual interest rate, usually compounded more than once per year.
  • Effective annual rate: the actual rate earned/paid in one year once compounding is taken into account — always ≥ the nominal rate when compounding more than once a year.
1 + i_eff = (1 + i_nom/m)m, where m = number of compounding periods per year

Basic Ratios & Special Angles

SOH – CAH – TOA
sinθ = Opposite/Hypotenuse, cosθ = Adjacent/Hypotenuse, tanθ = Opposite/Adjacent
  • Know the exact trig ratios for 0°, 30°, 45°, 60°, and 90° from memory (or derive from special triangles).

The CAST Diagram & Reduction Formulae

🔑 CAST diagram: shows which trig ratios are positive in each quadrant — All (Q1), Sine (Q2), Tan (Q3), Cosine (Q4).
  • Reduction formulae relate angles like (180°−θ), (180°+θ), (360°−θ), and (−θ) back to θ, using the CAST diagram to determine the sign.

Trigonometric Identities

tanθ = sinθ/cosθ
sin²θ + cos²θ = 1

Solving Trig Equations & General Solutions

  • Use a reference angle (found with a calculator) plus the CAST diagram to find all solutions in a given interval.
  • General solution: since trig functions are periodic, solutions repeat every 360° (or 180° for tan) — write the general solution using +k·360° (k is any integer).

Theorem of Pythagoras

In a right-angled triangle, the square of the hypotenuse equals the sum of the squares of the other two sides — used to prove right angles and calculate missing sides.

Similar Triangles

  • Triangles are similar if their corresponding angles are equal (AA) — corresponding sides are then in the same proportion.
  • Proportionality theorem: a line drawn parallel to one side of a triangle divides the other two sides proportionally.
🔑 Midpoint theorem: the line joining the midpoints of two sides of a triangle is parallel to the third side and equal to half its length.

Riders & Proofs

  • Always state the reason for every statement in a geometric proof — a correct answer without a reason loses marks.
  • Mark all given information (equal sides, equal angles, parallel lines) directly onto the diagram before starting a proof.
  • Build proofs step by step from what is given towards what needs to be proved — work backwards from the goal if you get stuck.

Core Formulas

Distance: d = √((x₂−x₁)² + (y₂−y₁)²)
Midpoint: M = ((x₁+x₂)/2 , (y₁+y₂)/2)
Gradient: m = (y₂−y₁)/(x₂−x₁)

Equation of a Straight Line

y = mx + c or y − y₁ = m(x − x₁)
  • Parallel lines have equal gradients (m₁ = m₂).
  • Perpendicular lines have gradients that multiply to −1 (m₁ × m₂ = −1).

Applying the Formulas

  • Use the distance formula to determine whether a triangle is isosceles, equilateral, or right-angled (via Pythagoras).
  • Use gradient to check whether three points are collinear (lie on the same straight line — all gradients between the points are equal).

Measures of Spread

Variance = average of squared deviations from the mean
Standard deviation = √Variance
  • A small standard deviation means data is clustered close to the mean; a large one means data is spread out.

Five-Number Summary & Box-and-Whisker Plots

  • Minimum, Q1 (lower quartile), Median (Q2), Q3 (upper quartile), Maximum.
  • Interquartile range (IQR) = Q3 − Q1 — measures the spread of the middle 50% of data, and is less affected by outliers than the range.

Scatter Plots & Correlation

  • A scatter plot shows the relationship between two numerical variables.
  • Positive correlation: both variables increase together. Negative correlation: one increases as the other decreases. No correlation: no clear pattern.
  • A line of best fit (drawn by eye) can be used to estimate values and describe the strength/direction of a relationship.

Ogives (Cumulative Frequency Graphs)

Plot cumulative frequency against the upper boundary of each class interval to estimate the median and quartiles from grouped data.

Exam Paper Guide

Understand exactly what the Grade 11 Mathematics papers look like and how to maximise your marks.

📄 Paper Overview

  • Paper 1: 2 hours, 100 marks — Algebra, equations & inequalities, patterns & sequences, functions & graphs, finance, growth & decay.
  • Paper 2: 2 hours, 100 marks — Statistics, analytical geometry, trigonometry, Euclidean geometry.

🔑 Levels of Questions

  • Level 1 (Knowledge): direct recall — apply a formula exactly as given.
  • Level 2 (Routine procedures): well-known procedures done in a familiar context.
  • Level 3 (Complex procedures): problems requiring several steps or making connections between topics.
  • Level 4 (Problem solving): non-routine problems requiring insight and reasoning.

Mark Allocation Guide

Question typeWhat earns marks
Calculation/formula questionCorrect formula stated, correct substitution, and correct final answer.
Multi-step problemMethod marks are awarded at each step, even if the final answer is wrong.
Geometric proof/riderEvery statement needs a valid reason — an unsupported correct answer loses marks.
📌 Exam tip: Never leave a question blank — write down the relevant formula and substitute what you know; method marks are often available even without a final answer.

Formula & Calculation Guide

Every key Grade 11 Mathematics formula in one place, with a worked example for each.

1

The Quadratic Formula

x = (−b ± √(b²−4ac)) / 2a

Example: Solve x² − 5x + 6 = 0.
a=1, b=−5, c=6
x = (5 ± √(25−24)) / 2 = (5 ± 1) / 2 → x = 3 or x = 2
2

Arithmetic Sequence — General Term

Tₙ = a + (n−1)d

Example: Sequence: 4, 9, 14, 19, ...
a=4, d=5. T₁₀ = 4 + (10−1)(5) = 4 + 45 = 49
3

Compound Growth

A = P(1 + i)ⁿ

Example: R8,000 invested at 9% compound interest for 3 years.
A = 8,000 × (1.09)³ = 8,000 × 1.295029 = R10,360.23
4

Distance & Midpoint (Analytical Geometry)

d = √((x₂−x₁)² + (y₂−y₁)²)
M = ((x₁+x₂)/2 , (y₁+y₂)/2)

Example: A(1,2) and B(7,10).
d = √((7−1)² + (10−2)²) = √(36+64) = √100 = 10
M = ((1+7)/2 , (2+10)/2) = (4, 6)
5

Trig Identity & Solving for θ

sin²θ + cos²θ = 1

Example: Solve sinθ = 0.5 for θ ∈ [0°, 360°].
Reference angle = 30°. Sine is positive in Q1 and Q2.
θ = 30° or θ = 150°
6

Standard Deviation (Statistics)

Standard deviation = √(average of squared deviations from the mean)

Example: Data set: 2, 4, 6, 8. Mean = 5.
Deviations: −3, −1, 1, 3 → squared: 9, 1, 1, 9 → average = 20/4 = 5
Standard deviation = √5 ≈ 2.24
