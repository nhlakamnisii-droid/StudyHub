Tsarist Russia & the Road to Revolution

Under Tsar Nicholas II, Russia was an autocracy with a vast, impoverished peasantry and a small, increasingly radicalised industrial working class.

  • 1905 Bloody Sunday: Troops fired on peaceful protesters marching to petition the Tsar — sparked the 1905 Revolution and forced limited reforms (the Duma).
  • Impact of WWI: Massive military losses, food shortages, and economic strain discredited the Tsar's government.

The 1917 Revolutions

  • Feb 1917 February Revolution: Mass protests and strikes forced Tsar Nicholas II to abdicate; a Provisional Government took over.
  • Oct 1917 October Revolution: The Bolsheviks, led by Vladimir Lenin, seized power under the slogan "Peace, Land, Bread."
🔑 Bolsheviks: A radical Marxist party led by Lenin that believed a small disciplined vanguard should lead the working class to revolution.

Civil War & Consolidation

  • Russian Civil War (1918–1921): Reds (Bolsheviks) fought Whites (anti-Bolshevik forces) — Reds won, securing Bolshevik control.
  • War Communism: Emergency policy of grain requisitioning and state control during the civil war.
  • New Economic Policy (NEP, 1921): Lenin allowed limited private trade and small-scale capitalism to revive the economy after the war.

Stalin's Rise & Rule

  • After Lenin's death in 1924, Joseph Stalin outmanoeuvred rivals (including Trotsky) to become sole leader.
  • Five-Year Plans: Rapid, state-directed industrialisation targets for heavy industry.
  • Collectivisation: Peasant farms forcibly merged into state-controlled collectives; resistance from "kulaks" (wealthier peasants) was crushed, contributing to widespread famine.
  • The Great Purge (1930s): Stalin eliminated real and perceived political rivals through show trials, executions, and the Gulag labour camp system.
🔑 Totalitarianism: A system in which the state seeks total control over political, economic, and social life — Stalin's USSR is a key example.

The Roaring Twenties

The 1920s USA saw rapid economic growth built on mass production, consumerism, and speculative investment.

  • Fordism: Henry Ford's assembly-line mass production lowered costs and enabled a consumer boom (cars, radios, household goods).
  • Stock market speculation: Many Americans bought shares "on margin" (with borrowed money), inflating share prices beyond real value.

The Wall Street Crash & Great Depression

  • Oct 1929 Wall Street Crash: Share prices collapsed, wiping out investors and triggering bank failures.
  • The Great Depression followed: mass unemployment, homelessness, and business closures throughout the 1930s.
  • The Dust Bowl — severe drought and poor farming practices — devastated agriculture in the Great Plains, worsening rural poverty.
🔑 Great Depression: The severe worldwide economic downturn of the 1930s, triggered by the 1929 Wall Street Crash.

Roosevelt's New Deal

  • Franklin D. Roosevelt (FDR) became president in 1933 and launched the New Deal — a programme of Relief, Recovery, and Reform.
  • Key agencies: the Civilian Conservation Corps (CCC, job creation), the Works Progress Administration (WPA, public works), and the Tennessee Valley Authority (TVA, infrastructure).
  • Social Security Act (1935): Introduced unemployment insurance and old-age pensions.
  • Critics on the right saw the New Deal as excessive government interference; critics on the left felt it did not go far enough to redistribute wealth.

Pseudo-Scientific Racism & Social Darwinism

From the late 1800s, some Europeans misapplied Darwin's theory of evolution to human societies, falsely claiming that races could be ranked in a hierarchy.

  • Social Darwinism: The false idea of "survival of the fittest" applied to races and nations, used to justify domination and inequality.
  • Eugenics movement: A pseudo-science advocating "selective breeding" to "improve" the human population — influenced policy in the USA, Britain, and Australia in the early 20th century.
🔑 Pseudo-science: Claims presented as scientific but lacking real evidence or rigorous method — used to justify racist ideology.

Consequences of Racial Ideology

  • These ideas underpinned imperialism and colonial rule, portraying colonised peoples as "inferior" to justify domination.
  • In Nazi Germany, eugenics and racial pseudo-science were taken to their most extreme conclusion, culminating in the Holocaust — the genocide of six million Jews and millions of others.
  • Racial ideology also shaped segregationist policy in the USA (Jim Crow laws) and laid ideological groundwork later drawn on in South Africa.

What Is Nationalism?

Nationalism is loyalty and devotion to one's nation — it has two faces: it can unify and liberate oppressed peoples, or it can exclude and oppress others.

  • Nationalism's origins lie in 19th-century Europe — e.g. the unification of Italy and Germany, and the revolutions of 1830 and 1848.
🔑 Nationalism: A political ideology based on the belief that a group sharing a common identity (language, culture, history) should govern itself as a nation.

Nationalism in Africa

  • European colonisation provoked African nationalism as a form of self-defence, unifying oppressed peoples against colonial rule.
  • African nationalist movements sought self-determination and independence from European empires.

Nationalism in South Africa

  • Afrikaner nationalism: Grew from a sense of Afrikaner identity and grievance, especially after the South African War (1899–1902); later became the ideological basis for apartheid.
  • African nationalism: The ANC (African National Congress) was founded in 1912 to unite Black South Africans and resist discriminatory laws.

Nationalism in the Middle East

  • Arab nationalism sought unity and independence from Ottoman and later European (British/French) control after WWI.
  • Zionism: A Jewish nationalist movement seeking a homeland in Palestine, gaining momentum after the Balfour Declaration (1917).
  • These competing nationalisms in the same territory sowed the seeds of long-running regional conflict.

The 1948 Election & the Birth of Apartheid

  • 1948 The National Party (NP) won the all-white general election on a platform of "apartheid" (separateness), building on earlier segregation laws.
🔑 Apartheid: An Afrikaans word meaning "apartness" — the system of legalised racial segregation and discrimination enforced in South Africa from 1948 to 1994.

Key Apartheid Legislation

  • Population Registration Act (1950): Classified every South African by race.
  • Group Areas Act (1950): Assigned racially separate residential and business areas, forcing removals.
  • Prohibition of Mixed Marriages Act (1949) & Immorality Act (1950): Banned interracial marriage and sexual relationships.
  • Bantu Education Act (1953): Created a separate, inferior education system for Black South Africans.
  • Pass Laws: Required Black South Africans to carry documents controlling their movement into "white" areas.
  • "Grand apartheid": The creation of ethnic "homelands" (Bantustans) intended to strip Black South Africans of their South African citizenship.

Resistance in the 1950s–60s

  • 1952 Defiance Campaign: The ANC and allies organised mass, deliberate defiance of unjust laws.
  • 1955 Freedom Charter: Adopted at the Congress of the People in Kliptown — declared "South Africa belongs to all who live in it."
  • 1960 Sharpeville Massacre: Police killed 69 people protesting the pass laws; the ANC and PAC were subsequently banned.
  • 1963–64 Rivonia Trial: Nelson Mandela and other ANC leaders were sentenced to life imprisonment for sabotage.

Exam Paper Guide

Understand exactly what the Grade 11 History paper looks like and how to maximise your marks.

📄 Paper Overview

  • Duration: 3 hours
  • Total marks: 150
  • Section A: Source-based questions
  • Section B: Essay question(s)

🔑 Source Question Command Words

  • Mention/Identify: 1 mark per fact, straight from the source.
  • Explain: Use the source AND your own knowledge to give reasons.
  • Compare: Identify similarities and/or differences between two sources.
  • Comment on/Evaluate: Assess the reliability, usefulness, or perspective of a source.

Mark Allocation Guide

Question typeMarks per pointWhat's expected
Mention/Identify1 markA single correct fact taken from the source.
Explain (source-based)2 marksA point supported by the source plus your own contextual knowledge.
Source comparison2–4 marksA clear similarity or difference, with evidence from both sources.
Essay50 marksA structured, argued response with intro, body paragraphs, and conclusion.
📌 Exam tip: Always read the source's origin, context, and content before answering — many marks are lost by ignoring who wrote a source and why.

Essay Writing Guide

A step-by-step technique for structuring a strong, high-marks History essay.

1

Unpack the Question

Identify the key concept being tested (e.g. causation, consequence, significance) and underline the specific dates/topic named in the question.

2

Write a Strong Introduction

  • Contextualise: Briefly describe the historical background (2–3 sentences).
  • Address the question: Directly respond to what is being asked.
  • State your argument: Your thesis — the overarching claim you will prove.
[Broad historical context — 2 sentences]. This essay will argue that [your thesis/main argument]. This will be demonstrated through an examination of [3 main points you will cover].
3

Develop Body Paragraphs (P.E.E.L Structure)

  • P — Point: State your argument for this paragraph clearly.
  • E — Evidence: Provide specific facts, dates, names, events to support your point.
  • E — Explain: Explain how your evidence proves your point.
  • L — Link: Connect back to the question/thesis.
[Topic sentence — your paragraph argument]. This is supported by the fact that [specific evidence: name, date, event]. This demonstrates [your explanation/analysis], which shows that [link back to question].
  • Aim for 3–4 body paragraphs with different arguments or factors.
  • Use historical vocabulary: causation, consequence, continuity, change, significance, perspective.
4

Write a Strong Conclusion

  • Summarise your main arguments (1 sentence each).
  • Restate your thesis in light of the evidence discussed.
  • Make a final judgement: Answer the question directly and decisively.
In conclusion, this essay has argued that [restate thesis]. This was demonstrated through [summary of main points]. Therefore, it is clear that [final answer to the question].

Marking Rubric — What the Examiner Looks For

SectionMarksCriteria
Introduction10Historical context present; question addressed; clear thesis stated
Evidence25Accurate and relevant facts, dates, events, and names used to support arguments
Analysis15Arguments explained and linked to the question; cause/effect discussed
Conclusion10Arguments summarised; thesis restated; clear, decisive answer
TOTAL50

Common Mistakes to Avoid

❌ Storytelling

Don't just narrate events. You must argue and analyse. Every paragraph should answer: So what? Why does this matter?

❌ Vague Statements

Avoid "many people suffered" — be specific: "69 protesters were killed at Sharpeville in 1960."

❌ Ignoring the Question

Re-read the question before every paragraph. Every point must connect back to what you are being asked.

❌ No Introduction/Conclusion

Many learners lose marks simply by starting with a body paragraph. Always write a proper intro and conclusion.

✍️ Practice tip: Time yourself writing one essay in 50 minutes. Then swap with a classmate and mark each other using the rubric above.
