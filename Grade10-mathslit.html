Study Notes

Working With Numbers

  • Be comfortable converting between fractions, decimals, and percentages — this is the foundation of almost every Math Lit question.
  • Rounding and estimating gives you a quick way to check whether a calculator answer is realistic.

Ratio & Rate

🔑 Ratio vs rate: a ratio compares two quantities of the same kind (e.g. 2 boys : 3 girls); a rate compares two quantities of different kinds (e.g. km/h, R/kg).

Percentages

Percentage = (Part ÷ Whole) × 100
New value after increase = Original × (1 + %/100)
New value after decrease = Original × (1 − %/100)

Calculator & Order of Operations

  • Always apply BODMAS: Brackets, Of/Orders, Division/Multiplication, Addition/Subtraction, working left to right within each level.

Describing a Relationship

  • A relationship between two quantities can be shown in four ways: in words, as a flow diagram, as a table of values, or as a formula/graph.

Direct & Indirect Proportion

🔑 Direct proportion: as one quantity increases, the other increases at the same rate (e.g. cost vs number of items). Indirect (inverse) proportion: as one quantity increases, the other decreases (e.g. speed vs time for a fixed distance).

Reading & Drawing Graphs

  • Identify the dependent variable (y-axis) and independent variable (x-axis) before reading any graph.
  • Always check the scale on each axis carefully — Math Lit graphs often use uneven or broken scales.

Personal & Household Budgets

  • A budget lists expected income and expenditure over a period, helping you plan and avoid overspending.
  • Income & expenditure statement: shows what was actually earned and spent, for comparison against the budget.

Payslips & Bank Statements

🔑 Gross income is your total earnings before deductions; net income (take-home pay) is what's left after tax, UIF, and other deductions are subtracted.

Tariff Systems

  • Flat rate tariff: a single fixed rate charged per unit, regardless of how much is used.
  • Sliding-scale (stepped) tariff: the rate increases as usage increases, common for municipal water and electricity billing.

Simple Interest

A = P(1 + i·n)
  • Interest is calculated only on the original principal (P) each year — the growth is the same fixed amount every period.

Compound Interest

A = P(1 + i)ⁿ
🔑 Key difference: compound interest is calculated on the accumulated amount each period, so savings and loans grow faster than with simple interest.

Hire Purchase & Loans

  • A hire purchase agreement lets you pay off an item in instalments, usually with interest added — always check the total amount repaid versus the cash price.

Exchange Rates

  • To convert Rand to a foreign currency, divide by the exchange rate; to convert foreign currency to Rand, multiply by the exchange rate.

Systems & Conversions

  • Learn the metric conversions: 1 km = 1000 m, 1 m = 100 cm, 1 kg = 1000 g, 1 l = 1000 ml.

Perimeter, Area & Volume

Rectangle: Area = l × b   Perimeter = 2(l+b)
Triangle: Area = ½ × base × height
Circle: Area = πr²   Circumference = 2πr
Rectangular prism: Volume = l × b × h

Temperature

🔑 Converting °C to °F: F = (C × 9/5) + 32.

Scale

🔑 Scale ratio (e.g. 1:50 000) means 1 unit on the map/plan represents 50 000 of the same unit in reality.
  • Actual distance = Map distance × scale factor.
  • A bar scale lets you measure distances directly with a ruler against the drawn scale bar.

Floor Plans & Scale Drawings

  • Floor plans use scale to represent real rooms and buildings on paper — use the given scale to calculate actual room dimensions or the plan dimensions of a real space.

Measures of Central Tendency

  • Mean: sum of all values ÷ number of values.
  • Median: the middle value when data is arranged in order.
  • Mode: the value that occurs most often.

Measures of Spread

  • Range = maximum value − minimum value.

Representing Data

  • Bar graphs compare categories; pie charts show proportions of a whole; line graphs show change over time.
🔑 Misleading graphs: watch for axes that don't start at zero, uneven intervals, or missing labels — these can distort how the data looks.

Expressing Probability

P(event) = Number of favourable outcomes ÷ Total number of outcomes
  • Probability can be written as a fraction, decimal, or percentage, and always lies between 0 (impossible) and 1 (certain).

Relative Frequency vs Theoretical Probability

🔑 Relative frequency is based on actual experimental results; theoretical probability is calculated from all possible outcomes. The more trials performed, the closer relative frequency gets to the theoretical value.

Exam Paper Guide

Understand exactly what the Grade 10 Mathematical Literacy papers look like and how to maximise your marks.

📄 Paper Overview

  • Paper 1: 1.5 hours, 100 marks — Numbers & calculations, patterns & relationships, finance.
  • Paper 2: 1.5 hours, 100 marks — Measurement, maps & plans, data handling, probability.

🔑 Levels of Questions

  • Level 1 (Knowing): straightforward calculations using given information.
  • Level 2 (Applying routine procedures): well-known procedures in familiar, everyday contexts.
  • Level 3 (Applying multi-step procedures): several steps or several data sources combined.
  • Level 4 (Reasoning & reflecting): interpreting, explaining, and justifying answers in context.

Mark Allocation Guide

Question typeWhat earns marks
Calculation questionCorrect formula/method shown, correct substitution, correct final answer with units.
Multi-step problemMethod marks are awarded at each step, even if the final answer is wrong.
Interpretation/explanation questionA full sentence linking your answer back to the context of the question — a number alone is not enough.
📌 Exam tip: Always include units (R, km, %, kg) in your final answer, and round money to two decimal places unless told otherwise — these small details cost easy marks.

Formula & Calculation Guide

Every key Grade 10 Mathematical Literacy formula in one place, with a worked example for each.

1

Percentage Increase

New value = Original × (1 + %/100)

Example: A R250 item increases by 12%.
New value = 250 × 1.12 = R280
2

Simple Interest

A = P(1 + i·n)

Example: R3,000 invested at 7% simple interest for 5 years.
A = 3,000 × (1 + 0.07×5) = 3,000 × 1.35 = R4,050
3

Compound Interest

A = P(1 + i)ⁿ

Example: R3,000 invested at 7% compound interest for 5 years.
A = 3,000 × (1.07)⁵ = 3,000 × 1.40255... = R4,207.66
4

Area of a Rectangle & Circle

Rectangle: A = l × b   Circle: A = πr²

Example: A rectangle 8 m by 5 m; a circle with radius 3 m.
Rectangle: A = 8 × 5 = 40 m²
Circle: A = π × 3² ≈ 28.27 m²
5

Using a Map Scale

Actual distance = Map distance × scale factor

Example: Scale is 1:50 000; map distance measures 4 cm.
Actual distance = 4 × 50,000 = 200,000 cm = 2 km
6

Currency Conversion

Foreign amount = Rand amount ÷ exchange rate

Example: Convert R2,000 to USD at an exchange rate of R18.50/$.
2,000 ÷ 18.50 ≈ $108.11
