Unit 1

DNA, Genetics & Inheritance

🧬 Paper 1
Structure of DNA ▾

DNA (Deoxyribonucleic acid) is a double helix made of two antiparallel strands wound around each other, discovered by Watson and Crick in 1953.

  • Nucleotide components: phosphate group + deoxyribose sugar + nitrogenous base
  • Purines: Adenine (A) and Guanine (G) — double-ring structures
  • Pyrimidines: Thymine (T) and Cytosine (C) — single-ring structures
  • Base pairing rules: A pairs with T (2 H-bonds); G pairs with C (3 H-bonds)
  • Strands are held together by hydrogen bonds between bases
  • Sugar-phosphate backbone forms through phosphodiester bonds
💡 Remember Chargaff's rule: %A = %T and %G = %C in any DNA molecule.
DNA Replication ▾

DNA replication is semi-conservative — each new DNA molecule contains one original strand and one new strand.

  • Helicase — unwinds and unzips the double helix by breaking hydrogen bonds
  • DNA Polymerase — adds complementary nucleotides to each template strand (5'→3' direction)
  • Leading strand — synthesized continuously toward the replication fork
  • Lagging strand — synthesized discontinuously as Okazaki fragments (away from fork)
  • DNA Ligase — joins Okazaki fragments together
  • Result: 2 identical DNA molecules from 1 original
ℹ️ Replication occurs during the S phase of the cell cycle, before mitosis or meiosis.
Protein Synthesis: Transcription & Translation ▾

The central dogma: DNA → RNA → Protein

Transcription (nucleus):

  • RNA polymerase unwinds DNA and reads the template strand (antisense strand)
  • Produces mRNA (messenger RNA) — uses uracil (U) instead of thymine
  • mRNA leaves the nucleus via nuclear pores

Translation (ribosome):

  • Ribosome reads mRNA in codons (triplets of bases)
  • tRNA brings specific amino acids; anticodon matches codon
  • Amino acids join by peptide bonds to form a polypeptide chain
  • Start codon: AUG (methionine); Stop codons: UAA, UAG, UGA
⚠️ Key distinction: Transcription makes RNA from DNA. Translation makes protein from RNA. Two separate processes, two locations.
Mutations ▾

A mutation is a change in the DNA sequence. Can be spontaneous or induced by mutagens (UV, chemicals, radiation).

Gene mutations (point mutations):

  • Substitution — one base replaced by another (may cause missense or nonsense mutation)
  • Insertion — extra base added; causes frameshift
  • Deletion — base removed; causes frameshift

Chromosomal mutations:

  • Non-disjunction — chromosomes fail to separate during meiosis → aneuploidy
  • Trisomy 21 (Down syndrome) — extra chromosome 21
Meiosis ▾

Meiosis is the cell division that produces haploid gametes (n) from diploid (2n) cells. Two divisions occur.

Meiosis I (reductive division):

  • Prophase I — crossing over (chiasmata formation)
  • Metaphase I — homologous pairs at equator
  • Anaphase I — homologs separate
  • Telophase I — 2 haploid cells

Meiosis II (equatorial division):

  • Like mitosis but in haploid cells
  • Sister chromatids separate
  • Result: 4 haploid cells (gametes)
🔑 Sources of genetic variation in meiosis: crossing over (prophase I) and independent assortment (metaphase I).
Mendelian Genetics & Inheritance ▾

Gregor Mendel's laws explain how traits are passed from parents to offspring.

  • Law of Segregation: alleles separate during gamete formation
  • Law of Independent Assortment: genes on different chromosomes assort independently
  • Dominant allele (uppercase) masks the recessive allele (lowercase)
  • Genotype = allele combination (AA, Aa, aa); Phenotype = observable trait
  • Monohybrid cross: Aa × Aa → 1AA : 2Aa : 1aa (3:1 phenotype ratio)
  • Dihybrid cross: AaBb × AaBb → 9:3:3:1 phenotype ratio

Extensions to Mendel:

  • Incomplete dominance: heterozygote shows blended phenotype (e.g. RR=red, rr=white, Rr=pink)
  • Co-dominance: both alleles fully expressed (e.g. ABO blood groups — IAIB)
  • Sex-linked traits: genes on X chromosome (haemophilia, colour blindness)
  • Polygenic inheritance: multiple genes control one trait (e.g. skin colour)
⚠️ Pedigree diagrams: squares = males, circles = females, filled = affected. Know how to determine genotypes and predict offspring ratios.
Genetic Technology ▾
  • Gel electrophoresis: separates DNA fragments by size using electric current through agarose gel
  • DNA fingerprinting (profiling): uses STRs (short tandem repeats) for identification — forensics, paternity testing
  • PCR (Polymerase Chain Reaction): amplifies tiny DNA samples; uses heat-stable Taq polymerase
  • Recombinant DNA technology: restriction enzymes cut DNA; ligase joins it into plasmid vectors
  • GMOs: transgenic organisms with genes from other species (Bt crops, insulin production)
  • Genetic screening: testing embryos or individuals for genetic conditions
ℹ️ Ethical issues: privacy of genetic info, GMO safety, access to technology, patenting genes.
Unit 2

Evolution

🦴 Paper 1
Darwin's Theory of Natural Selection ▾

Charles Darwin proposed evolution by natural selection — the mechanism by which organisms best adapted to their environment survive and reproduce.

Key postulates:

  • Overproduction — organisms produce more offspring than can survive
  • Variation — individuals in a population show heritable variation
  • Competition (struggle for existence) — limited resources cause competition
  • Survival of the fittest — organisms best adapted survive to reproduce
  • Inheritance — favourable traits passed to offspring over generations
💡 Lamarck's theory (use and disuse, inheritance of acquired characteristics) was WRONG — acquired traits cannot be inherited genetically.
Evidence for Evolution ▾
Type of EvidenceExplanation
Fossil recordShows progression of organisms over time; transitional fossils link ancestral and modern forms
Comparative anatomyHomologous structures (same origin, different function) e.g. human arm, bat wing, whale flipper
Analogous structuresSame function, different origin — convergent evolution (e.g. bird and insect wings)
Vestigial structuresRemnant organs with no current function (e.g. human appendix, coccyx, wisdom teeth)
Comparative embryologySimilar embryonic stages across vertebrates
BiogeographyDistribution of species matches evolutionary history (e.g. Galápagos finches)
BiochemistrySimilar DNA/protein sequences indicate shared ancestry
Types of Selection & Speciation ▾

Types of natural selection:

  • Directional selection — one extreme phenotype favoured; shifts population mean
  • Stabilising selection — intermediate phenotype favoured; reduces variation
  • Disruptive selection — both extreme phenotypes favoured; increases variation

Speciation:

  • Allopatric speciation — geographic isolation → populations diverge → reproductive isolation → new species
  • Sympatric speciation — new species forms within the same geographic area
  • Reproductive isolation is essential for speciation
ℹ️ Gene flow (migration between populations) reduces genetic differences. Genetic drift causes random changes in allele frequency in small populations.
Human Evolution ▾

Humans belong to the family Hominidae. Africa is considered the "cradle of humankind" — especially South Africa and East Africa.

Key characteristics of human evolution:

  • Bipedalism — walking upright on two legs (frees hands, more efficient)
  • Reduced brow ridges and prognathism over time
  • Increasing brain size (cranial capacity)
  • Development of language and tool use
  • Reduced jaw size and dental differences

Key hominid species (oldest → newest):

SpeciesKey features
Australopithecus africanusSmall brain (~450 cc), bipedal, ape-like face; found in SA (Taung child, Mrs Ples)
Homo habilis"Handy man"; first tool use; brain ~600–700 cc
Homo ergaster/erectusFirst to leave Africa; controlled fire; brain ~900 cc
Homo sapiensModern humans; brain ~1350 cc; complex language and culture
🦴 SA sites: Sterkfontein, Swartkrans, Kromdraai (Cradle of Humankind, Gauteng) — UNESCO World Heritage Site.
Unit 3

Reproduction

🔬 Paper 1
Asexual vs Sexual Reproduction ▾

Asexual reproduction:

  • One parent only
  • Offspring genetically identical (clones)
  • Rapid, energy-efficient
  • No genetic variation
  • Examples: budding (yeast), binary fission (bacteria), vegetative propagation (plants), fragmentation

Sexual reproduction:

  • Two parents, fusion of gametes
  • Genetic variation in offspring
  • Slower, energy-costly
  • Better long-term adaptability
  • Involves meiosis to form gametes
Plant Reproduction ▾

Flower structure (angiosperm reproduction):

  • Stamen (male): anther (pollen) + filament
  • Carpel/Pistil (female): stigma + style + ovary (contains ovules)
  • Pollination: transfer of pollen to stigma (wind, insects, water, birds)
  • Fertilisation: pollen tube grows through style → sperm nuclei reach ovule
  • Double fertilisation: one sperm + egg = zygote; one sperm + polar nuclei = endosperm

Fruit and seed dispersal: wind (dandelion), water (coconut), animals (berries, hooks), self-dispersal (explosive pods)

Seed germination conditions: water, oxygen, suitable temperature (NOT light for most seeds)

Human Reproduction — Male System ▾
  • Testes — produce sperm (spermatogenesis) and testosterone
  • Epididymis — sperm maturation and storage
  • Vas deferens — tube carrying sperm to urethra
  • Seminal vesicles — fructose-rich fluid for sperm energy
  • Prostate gland — alkaline fluid to neutralise vaginal acidity
  • Cowper's gland — pre-ejaculatory fluid
  • Penis — delivers sperm into female reproductive tract
  • Semen = sperm + seminal fluid (~200–400 million sperm per ejaculation)
ℹ️ Spermatogenesis occurs in seminiferous tubules within the testes and requires a temperature slightly lower than body temperature (hence external scrotum).
Human Reproduction — Female System ▾
  • Ovaries — produce ova (oogenesis) and hormones (oestrogen, progesterone)
  • Fallopian tubes (oviducts) — carry ovum from ovary to uterus; fertilisation occurs here
  • Uterus — site of implantation and fetal development; endometrium is the lining
  • Cervix — lower end of uterus; dilates during birth
  • Vagina — birth canal; receives penis during intercourse

Menstrual cycle (28 days):

  • Day 1–5: Menstruation — endometrium shed
  • Day 6–13: Follicular phase — FSH stimulates follicle; oestrogen rises
  • Day 14: Ovulation — LH surge triggers release of ovum
  • Day 15–28: Luteal phase — corpus luteum produces progesterone; endometrium thickens
🔑 Hormones: FSH (follicle stimulation), LH (ovulation trigger), Oestrogen (endometrium growth), Progesterone (maintains endometrium).
Fertilisation, Pregnancy & Birth ▾
  • Fertilisation: sperm meets ovum in fallopian tube → zygote (2n)
  • Implantation: blastocyst embeds in endometrium (day ~7)
  • Placenta: exchange organ between mother and fetus; hCG hormone maintains corpus luteum early in pregnancy
  • Umbilical cord: connects fetus to placenta (2 arteries + 1 vein)
  • Amniotic fluid: protects fetus from shock; maintains temperature
  • Birth (parturition): oxytocin stimulates uterine contractions

Contraception methods:

  • Hormonal (pill, injection, implant) — prevent ovulation
  • Barrier (condom, diaphragm) — prevent sperm reaching egg; condom also prevents STIs
  • IUD — prevents implantation
  • Abstinence — 100% effective
  • Sterilisation (vasectomy/tubal ligation) — permanent
Unit 4

Nervous System & Homeostasis

🧠 Paper 2
Neurons & Nerve Impulses ▾

Types of neurons:

  • Sensory (afferent) — carry impulse from receptors TO CNS
  • Motor (efferent) — carry impulse FROM CNS to effectors (muscles, glands)
  • Interneurons (relay) — found within the CNS; connect sensory and motor neurons

Neuron structure:

  • Cell body (soma) + dendrites (receive signals) + axon (transmit signals) + axon terminals
  • Myelin sheath (formed by Schwann cells) — speeds up impulse conduction
  • Nodes of Ranvier — gaps in myelin where impulse jumps (saltatory conduction)

Action potential:

  • Resting potential: inside of neuron is negative (−70 mV); maintained by Na⁺/K⁺ pump
  • Depolarisation: Na⁺ rushes in → inside becomes positive
  • Repolarisation: K⁺ rushes out → returns to resting potential
  • All-or-nothing principle: impulse either fires fully or not at all
Synapses ▾

A synapse is a junction between two neurons where chemical transmission occurs.

  • Nerve impulse reaches pre-synaptic terminal
  • Synaptic vesicles release neurotransmitters into synaptic cleft
  • Neurotransmitters bind to receptors on post-synaptic membrane
  • New impulse generated in post-synaptic neuron (or inhibited)
  • Neurotransmitters broken down (by enzymes) or reabsorbed

Common neurotransmitters:

  • Acetylcholine (ACh) — neuromuscular junction, parasympathetic
  • Dopamine — reward, motivation (affected by drugs)
  • Serotonin — mood, sleep
  • Noradrenaline — fight or flight
⚠️ Impulses travel in ONE direction across synapse — from pre-synaptic to post-synaptic only.
The Brain & Spinal Cord (CNS) ▾
StructureFunction
CerebrumThinking, memory, voluntary movement, senses, language; divided into lobes
CerebellumBalance, coordination, fine motor control
Medulla oblongataControls involuntary functions: heart rate, breathing, swallowing
HypothalamusHomeostasis, body temp, hunger, thirst, links nervous & endocrine systems
Spinal cordPathway for impulses; controls reflex arcs

Reflex arc: Receptor → Sensory neuron → Spinal cord (interneuron) → Motor neuron → Effector

ℹ️ Reflex arcs bypass the brain for speed — that's why you pull your hand away from heat before you feel the pain.

🔑 Hormones travel in blood (slow, long-lasting). Nerve impulses travel along neurons (fast, short-lived).
Drugs & Their Effects on the Nervous System ▾

Drugs alter the normal functioning of synapses by mimicking, blocking, or altering neurotransmitter action.

Depressants:

  • Slow CNS activity
  • Examples: alcohol, tranquilisers, heroin
  • Increase GABA / reduce nerve impulse rate

Stimulants:

  • Speed up CNS activity
  • Examples: caffeine, cocaine, nicotine, tik (meth)
  • Increase dopamine / noradrenaline

Hallucinogens: distort perception (e.g. cannabis, LSD)

Addiction occurs because drugs overstimulate the brain's reward pathway — dopamine floods synapses and the brain compensates by reducing natural receptor sensitivity.

⚠️ Exam context: Be able to explain WHY a specific drug causes a specific effect at the synapse level.
Homeostasis ▾

Homeostasis is the maintenance of a stable internal environment despite external changes. Uses negative feedback loops.

Blood glucose regulation:

  • High glucose → pancreas secretes insulin → cells absorb glucose → glucose converted to glycogen (liver/muscle)
  • Low glucose → pancreas secretes glucagon → glycogen converted back to glucose (glycogenolysis)
  • Diabetes Type 1: pancreas cannot produce insulin
  • Diabetes Type 2: cells become insulin-resistant

Thermoregulation (body temp ~37°C):

  • Too hot: vasodilation, sweating, hair lies flat
  • Too cold: vasoconstriction, shivering, hair stands up (goosebumps), less sweating

Osmoregulation (water balance): kidneys regulate water/salt balance; ADH increases water reabsorption

Unit 5

Ecology & Human Impact

🌿 Paper 2
Ecological Concepts ▾
  • Ecology — study of interactions between organisms and their environment
  • Ecosystem — community of organisms + their abiotic environment
  • Biome — large-scale ecosystem defined by climate and dominant vegetation
  • Population — all individuals of one species in an area
  • Community — all populations in an ecosystem
  • Habitat — physical place where organism lives
  • Niche — organism's role in the ecosystem (what it does, not where it lives)
  • Abiotic factors: temperature, water, light, pH, wind, soil type
  • Biotic factors: predation, competition, disease, parasitism, mutualism
Energy Flow & Food Webs ▾

Trophic levels:

  • Level 1: Producers (plants, algae) — photosynthesise to make food
  • Level 2: Primary consumers — herbivores
  • Level 3: Secondary consumers — carnivores eating herbivores
  • Level 4: Tertiary consumers — top predators
  • Decomposers — bacteria and fungi breaking down dead matter

10% rule: Only ~10% of energy transfers from one trophic level to the next (rest lost as heat/metabolic processes)

Food chain: grass → grasshopper → frog → snake → eagle

🔑 Ecological pyramids: Pyramid of numbers, biomass, and energy — explain why food chains rarely exceed 4–5 levels.
Population Ecology ▾

Population growth:

  • J-curve (exponential growth): unlimited resources, no predators — population explodes
  • S-curve (logistic growth): reaches carrying capacity (K) — limiting factors slow growth

Limiting factors:

  • Density-dependent: disease, predation, competition (increase with population size)
  • Density-independent: drought, flood, fire, pesticide (affect regardless of size)

Species interactions:

TypeSpecies ASpecies BExample
Mutualism++Bee + flower, oxpecker + rhino
Commensalism+0Orchid on tree
Parasitism+−Tick on dog
Predation+−Lion eats zebra
Competition−−Lions and hyenas
Nutrient Cycles ▾

Carbon cycle:

  • Photosynthesis removes CO₂ from atmosphere
  • Respiration, combustion, decomposition release CO₂
  • Fossil fuels are carbon stores — burning them increases atmospheric CO₂

Nitrogen cycle:

  • Nitrogen fixation: N₂ → NH₃ (by Rhizobium bacteria in legume root nodules or lightning)
  • Nitrification: NH₃ → NO₂⁻ → NO₃⁻ (by nitrifying bacteria)
  • Assimilation: plants absorb NO₃⁻ and build proteins
  • Denitrification: NO₃⁻ → N₂ (by denitrifying bacteria — reduces soil fertility)
  • Decomposition: dead organisms release NH₃
Human Impact & Conservation ▾

Human impacts on the environment:

  • Deforestation — habitat loss, soil erosion, increased CO₂
  • Pollution — water (eutrophication), air (smog, acid rain), soil (pesticides)
  • Eutrophication: excess nutrients → algal bloom → O₂ depletion → fish kill
  • Ozone depletion — CFCs destroy ozone layer; increases UV radiation
  • Global warming — greenhouse gases (CO₂, CH₄, N₂O) trap heat
  • Overexploitation — overfishing, poaching, overhunting
  • Invasive species — out-compete native species (e.g. Black Wattle, Trout)

Conservation strategies:

  • In-situ: national parks, game reserves, marine protected areas
  • Ex-situ: zoos, botanical gardens, seed banks, captive breeding
  • Legislation: CITES, Biodiversity Act, protected species lists
  • Sustainable use: regulated hunting, responsible fishing
🌍 SA context: Kruger National Park, uKhahlamba Drakensberg Park, iSimangaliso Wetland Park (KZN) — know your biomes and conservation areas.
