GAAP Principles

  • Historical cost: assets are recorded at their original purchase price.
  • Prudence: don't overstate income/assets or understate expenses/liabilities.
  • Matching: expenses are recorded in the same period as the income they helped generate.
  • Business entity rule: the business's finances are kept completely separate from the owner's personal finances.

The Accounting Equation

Assets = Owner's Equity + Liabilities
🔑 Every transaction affects at least two accounts and must keep the accounting equation in balance — this is the foundation of double-entry accounting.
  • Assets: things the business owns (e.g. equipment, vehicles, bank, debtors).
  • Liabilities: what the business owes to others (e.g. creditors, loans).
  • Owner's equity: the owner's claim on the business — capital + income − expenses − drawings.

What Are Source Documents?

The original paper or digital records that prove a transaction took place — every entry in the accounting records must be backed by a source document.

Common Source Documents

  • Invoice: issued for a credit sale/purchase of goods.
  • Receipt: proof that cash has been received.
  • Cheque counterfoil/EFT proof: proof of a payment made.
  • Cash register roll: summary of daily cash sales.
  • Credit note: issued when goods are returned by a customer (reduces what they owe).
  • Debit note: issued when goods are returned to a supplier (reduces what the business owes).

Books of First Entry

Transactions are first recorded in a subsidiary journal (grouped by type) before being posted to the general ledger.

  • Cash Receipts Journal (CRJ): records all cash received.
  • Cash Payments Journal (CPJ): records all cash paid out.
  • Debtors Journal (DJ): records credit sales.
  • Creditors Journal (CJ): records credit purchases.
  • Debtors Allowances Journal (DAJ): records goods returned by debtors.
  • Creditors Allowances Journal (CAJ): records goods returned to creditors.
  • General Journal (GJ): records transactions that don't fit any other journal (e.g. correcting entries).
🔑 Why use journals first? Grouping similar transactions together makes posting to the ledger faster and reduces errors.

Posting to the Ledger

Every amount in a subsidiary journal is transferred ("posted") to its relevant account in the general ledger, which groups all transactions by account.

Debit & Credit Rules

Assets & Expenses: increase = Debit, decrease = Credit
Liabilities, Equity & Income: increase = Credit, decrease = Debit
  • Each ledger account has a debit (left) side and a credit (right) side — the difference between the two sides is the account's balance.
  • Ledger accounts are grouped into the General Ledger (asset, liability, equity accounts) and Nominal Ledger (income and expense accounts).

Purpose of a Trial Balance

A list of all ledger account balances at a specific date, used to check that total debits equal total credits before preparing financial statements.

🔑 A balanced trial balance does not guarantee there are no errors — some errors (like a transaction posted to the wrong account) won't unbalance the totals.

Common Errors to Check

  • Transposition errors (digits swapped, e.g. R450 written as R540).
  • Omission (a transaction left out entirely).
  • Posting to the wrong account or the wrong side of an account.

What Is VAT?

An indirect tax added to the price of most goods and services, collected by the business on behalf of the government (SARS).

VAT amount = Price (excl. VAT) × VAT rate
  • Input VAT: VAT paid on purchases (claimed back from SARS).
  • Output VAT: VAT charged on sales (owed to SARS).
🔑 VAT payable/refundable = Output VAT − Input VAT. If output exceeds input, the business owes SARS; if input exceeds output, SARS owes the business.

Why Reconcile?

The business's own Cash Journal balance and the bank statement balance rarely match exactly on a given date — a bank reconciliation explains the difference.

Common Reconciling Items

  • Outstanding deposits: deposited by the business but not yet reflected on the bank statement.
  • Outstanding cheques: issued by the business but not yet presented to the bank for payment.
  • Bank charges/interest: appear on the bank statement but are only recorded in the business's books once identified.
🔑 Debit and credit balances on a bank statement work in reverse from the business's own books, since the bank statement is written from the bank's point of view.

Why Ethics Matters

Accurate, honest financial records protect the business, its owners, and anyone else who relies on the information (e.g. banks, SARS, investors).

Common Unethical Practices

  • Recording fictitious transactions to inflate income or hide losses.
  • Misappropriation of cash or stock by employees or owners.
  • Failing to declare all income for tax purposes.
🔑 Internal control measures — like requiring two signatures on cheques, or separating who authorises a payment from who processes it — help prevent and detect unethical behaviour.

Exam Paper Guide

Understand exactly what the Grade 10 Accounting papers look like and how to maximise your marks.

📄 Paper Overview

  • Typically one paper of around 2 hours, made up of several questions covering GAAP, the accounting equation, journals, ledgers, trial balance, VAT, and bank reconciliation.
  • Questions usually combine short theory sections with practical recording and calculation tasks.

🔑 Levels of Questions

  • Level 1 (Knowledge): stating a definition or a GAAP principle.
  • Level 2 (Comprehension/Application): recording a transaction in the correct journal or ledger account.
  • Level 3 (Analysis): preparing a trial balance or bank reconciliation from given data.
  • Level 4 (Synthesis/Evaluation): identifying and correcting errors, or commenting on ethical issues.

Mark Allocation Guide

Question typeWhat earns marks
Journal entryCorrect account names, correct amounts, and correct debit/credit placement.
Ledger postingCorrect account, correct side (debit/credit), and a running or closing balance where required.
Calculation (e.g. VAT, bank reconciliation)Correct formula/method shown, correct substitution, and correct final figure.
📌 Exam tip: Always show your workings for calculations — method marks are awarded even if your final figure is incorrect.

Formula & Calculation Guide

Every key Grade 10 Accounting formula in one place, with a worked example for each.

1

The Accounting Equation

Assets = Owner's Equity + Liabilities

Example: A business has assets of R80,000 and liabilities of R20,000.
Owner's Equity = 80,000 − 20,000 = R60,000
2

Owner's Equity (Expanded)

Owner's Equity = Capital + Income − Expenses − Drawings

Example: Capital R50,000, Income R20,000, Expenses R8,000, Drawings R2,000.
Owner's Equity = 50,000 + 20,000 − 8,000 − 2,000 = R60,000
3

Calculating VAT

VAT amount = Price (excl. VAT) × VAT rate

Example: Goods priced at R1,000 (excl. VAT), VAT rate 15%.
VAT = 1,000 × 0.15 = R150. Price incl. VAT = R1,150
4

VAT Payable / Refundable

VAT payable = Output VAT − Input VAT

Example: Output VAT R4,500, Input VAT R3,200.
VAT payable = 4,500 − 3,200 = R1,300 owed to SARS
5

Bank Reconciliation — Adjusted Balance

Adjusted Cash Journal balance = Unadjusted balance ± items not yet recorded (e.g. bank charges, interest)

Example: Cash Journal balance R5,000, bank charges R80 not yet recorded.
Adjusted balance = 5,000 − 80 = R4,920
6

Checking a Trial Balance

Total Debits must equal Total Credits

Example: Total debit column = R124,500. Total credit column = R124,500.
Since both totals match, the trial balance balances.
