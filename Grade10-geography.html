Structure of the Atmosphere

  • The atmosphere is layered: troposphere (weather occurs here), stratosphere (contains the ozone layer), mesosphere, and thermosphere.

Composition of Air

🔑 Air composition: approximately 78% nitrogen, 21% oxygen, and 1% other gases including carbon dioxide and water vapour.

Weather vs Climate

  • Weather is the day-to-day state of the atmosphere; climate is the average weather conditions of a place over a long period (usually 30+ years).

Elements of Weather

  • Temperature, precipitation, humidity, air pressure, and wind are the core elements measured to describe weather.

Instruments

  • Thermometer: measures temperature. Rain gauge: measures precipitation. Barometer: measures air pressure. Anemometer: measures wind speed. Hygrometer: measures humidity.
🔑 Reading a synoptic weather map: isobars (lines of equal pressure) close together indicate strong winds; widely spaced isobars indicate calmer conditions.

Pressure Systems

  • A high-pressure system (anticyclone) brings settled, clear weather; a low-pressure system (cyclone) brings unsettled, cloudy, and rainy weather.

How Wind Forms

  • Wind is air moving from areas of high pressure to areas of low pressure; the greater the pressure difference, the stronger the wind.

Stages of the Water Cycle

  • Evaporation → condensation → precipitation → run-off/infiltration → back to evaporation, in a continuous cycle.

Types of Rainfall

  • Convectional rainfall: caused by intense surface heating that forces air to rise and cool.
  • Orographic (relief) rainfall: occurs when moist air is forced to rise over a mountain range.
  • Frontal (cyclonic) rainfall: occurs where warm and cold air masses meet.

Rock Types

  • Igneous rock: formed from cooled magma or lava (e.g. granite). Sedimentary rock: formed from compressed layers of sediment (e.g. sandstone). Metamorphic rock: formed when existing rock is changed by heat and pressure (e.g. marble from limestone).

Weathering

🔑 Mechanical weathering breaks rock apart physically (e.g. freeze-thaw); chemical weathering changes the rock's chemical composition (e.g. oxidation); biological weathering is caused by living organisms (e.g. plant roots).

Population Concepts

  • Population distribution: where people live. Population density: the number of people per square kilometre.

Migration

🔑 Push factors drive people away from an area (e.g. drought, unemployment); pull factors attract people to a new area (e.g. job opportunities, better services).
  • Rural-urban migration is the movement of people from rural areas to cities, a major trend in South Africa.

Types of Settlement

  • Rural settlements are small, dispersed, and often based on agriculture; urban settlements are larger, denser, and based on industry and services.

Settlement Patterns

  • Nucleated: clustered closely together. Dispersed: spread out over an area. Linear: stretched along a route such as a road or river.

Types of Maps

  • Topographic maps show natural and human features of an area at a specific scale, using contour lines to represent relief.
  • Orthophoto maps are aerial photographs with grid lines, used alongside topographic maps to identify features.

Grid References & Direction

🔑 Four-figure grid reference: read along the x-axis (easting) first, then up the y-axis (northing) — "read right up".

Exam Paper Guide

Understand exactly what the Grade 10 Geography papers look like and how to maximise your marks.

📄 Paper Structure

  • Paper 1: Theory paper covering physical and human geography topics, with source-based questions using photographs, diagrams, and maps.
  • Paper 2: Map work paper, focused on topographic map and orthophoto map skills.

🔑 Common Command Words

  • Identify/Name: give a specific term or feature.
  • Describe: give a detailed account of what something looks like or how it happens.
  • Explain: give reasons for why something happens.
  • Calculate: use map work skills (e.g. scale, area) to work out a numerical answer.

Mark Allocation Guide

Question typeWhat earns marks
Map work calculationCorrect method shown (e.g. scale conversion) and a final answer with correct units.
Describe/Explain questionOne mark per distinct, correctly developed point — vague or repeated points don't earn extra marks.
Source-based questionReading the given photograph, diagram, or map accurately before answering.
📌 Exam tip: For map work calculations, always show your working — method marks are awarded even if your final answer is slightly off due to a measuring error.

Map Skills Guide

Core map work techniques with worked examples.

1

Calculating Distance Using Scale

Actual distance = Map distance × scale factor

Example: Scale is 1:50,000; map distance measures 6 cm.
Actual distance = 6 × 50,000 = 300,000 cm = 3 km
2

Four-Figure Grid References

Read along the x-axis (easting), then up the y-axis (northing) — "read right, then up".

Example: A feature sits in the grid square where easting 34 meets northing 21 → grid reference 3421.
3

Determining Direction (Bearings)

Place the centre of a compass over the starting point; read the angle clockwise from north to the destination point.

Example: Point B lies directly south-east of Point A → bearing = 135°
4

Reading Contour Lines

Closely spaced contour lines indicate steep slopes; widely spaced contour lines indicate gentle slopes.

Example: Contours 40 m apart in a small horizontal distance = a steep slope
5

Calculating Area on a Map

Area = length × breadth (using the map's grid squares or scale)

Example: A dam covers 2 grid squares, each representing 1 km × 1 km.
Area = 2 × (1 × 1) = 2 km²
