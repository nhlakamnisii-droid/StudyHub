Laws of Exponents

am·an = am+n   am÷an = am−n
(am)n = amn   a0 = 1 (a≠0)   a−n = 1/aⁿ
  • Apply exponent laws to simplify expressions with the same base before doing anything else.

Simplifying Surds

🔑 Simplifying a surd: extract the largest perfect square factor, e.g. √50 = √(25×2) = 5√2.
  • Only "like surds" (same number under the root) can be added or subtracted directly.

Expanding & Factorising

  • Expanding: use the distributive law and FOIL for binomials, e.g. (x+2)(x+3) = x²+5x+6.
  • Common factor: always take out the highest common factor first.
  • Difference of two squares: a² − b² = (a−b)(a+b).
  • Trinomials (a=1): find two numbers that multiply to give "c" and add to give "b" in x²+bx+c.
  • Grouping: used for four-term expressions with no single common factor.

Linear Equations

  • Solve by getting all terms with the variable on one side and constants on the other, then divide by the coefficient.

Quadratic Equations (by Factorisation)

🔑 Zero product rule: if (x−p)(x−q) = 0, then x=p or x=q — a product can only be zero if one of its factors is zero.
  • Always set the equation equal to zero first, then factorise, then apply the zero product rule.

Simultaneous Equations

  • Solve two linear equations in two unknowns using substitution (make one variable the subject, substitute into the other equation) or elimination (add/subtract equations to remove one variable).

Linear Inequalities

  • Solved the same way as linear equations — but if you multiply or divide both sides by a negative number, the inequality sign must be reversed.
  • Solutions are represented on a number line with an open circle (strict, < or >) or closed circle (inclusive, ≤ or ≥).

Describing Patterns

  • Patterns can be described using words, flow diagrams, or tables of values before finding a formula.
  • Always write out the first few terms and the differences between consecutive terms first.

Linear (Arithmetic) Number Patterns

Tₙ = a + (n−1)d
  • A pattern has a constant common difference (d) between consecutive terms — this makes it linear.
  • "a" is the first term; "n" is the term's position in the sequence.
🔑 Checking your formula: always substitute n=1, n=2, n=3 back into your general term — it must reproduce the original sequence exactly.

The Linear Function

y = mx + c
  • "m" is the gradient (steepness); "c" is the y-intercept.
  • If m>0 the line increases left to right; if m<0 it decreases.

The Parabola

y = ax² + q
  • Turning point at (0,q); axis of symmetry is the y-axis (x=0).
  • If a>0 the parabola opens upward (minimum turning point); if a<0 it opens downward (maximum turning point).

The Hyperbola

y = a/x + q
  • Vertical asymptote at x=0; horizontal asymptote at y=q.

The Exponential Function

y = a·bx + q   (b > 0, b ≠ 1)
  • Horizontal asymptote at y=q. If b>1 the function increases; if 0<b<1 it decreases.
  • Always identify intercepts and asymptotes first when sketching any graph.

Basic Ratios in a Right-Angled Triangle

SOH – CAH – TOA
sinθ = Opposite/Hypotenuse, cosθ = Adjacent/Hypotenuse, tanθ = Opposite/Adjacent

Special Angles

🔑 Know these from memory: the exact trig ratios for 0°, 30°, 45°, 60°, and 90° — they appear constantly in exams.

Solving Right-Angled Triangles

  • Use the ratio that connects the sides/angle you know to the one you need to find.
  • Angle of elevation: the angle looking upward from the horizontal. Angle of depression: the angle looking downward from the horizontal.

The Basic Identity & Trig Graphs

tanθ = sinθ/cosθ
  • The graphs of sinθ and cosθ have amplitude 1 and period 360°; the graph of tanθ has period 180° and asymptotes at 90° and 270°.

Lines & Angles

  • When a line cuts two parallel lines: corresponding angles are equal, alternate angles are equal, and co-interior angles add up to 180°.

Triangles

  • The interior angles of a triangle always add up to 180°.
  • Exterior angle theorem: an exterior angle of a triangle equals the sum of the two interior opposite angles.
  • An isosceles triangle has two equal sides and the angles opposite those sides are equal.
🔑 Congruency conditions: two triangles are congruent if SSS, SAS, AAS, or RHS matches — all corresponding sides and angles are then equal.

Quadrilaterals

  • Parallelogram: opposite sides parallel & equal, opposite angles equal, diagonals bisect each other.
  • Rectangle: a parallelogram with four right angles and equal diagonals.
  • Rhombus: a parallelogram with four equal sides; diagonals bisect each other at right angles.
  • Square: has all the properties of both a rectangle and a rhombus.
  • Kite: two pairs of adjacent equal sides; one diagonal bisects the other at right angles.

Core Analytical Geometry Formulas

Distance: d = √((x₂−x₁)² + (y₂−y₁)²)
Midpoint: M = ((x₁+x₂)/2 , (y₁+y₂)/2)
Gradient: m = (y₂−y₁)/(x₂−x₁)
  • Use these formulas together with the equation y=mx+c to find the equation of a line through two given points.

Simple Interest

A = P(1 + i·n)
  • Interest is calculated only on the original principal (P) each period — the amount grows by the same fixed amount every year.

Compound Interest

A = P(1 + i)ⁿ
🔑 Key difference: compound interest is calculated on the accumulated amount each period (interest earns interest), so it grows faster than simple interest over time.
  • P = principal, i = interest rate per period (as a decimal), n = number of periods.

Measures of Central Tendency

  • Mean: sum of all values ÷ number of values.
  • Median: the middle value when data is arranged in order.
  • Mode: the value that occurs most often.

Measures of Dispersion

  • Range = maximum − minimum.
  • Five-number summary: minimum, Q1 (lower quartile), median, Q3 (upper quartile), maximum — used to draw a box-and-whisker plot.

Frequency Tables & Histograms

Grouped data is organised into class intervals with a frequency count, then displayed as a histogram (bars with no gaps, since the data is continuous).

Probability

P(A or B) = P(A) + P(B) − P(A and B)
  • A Venn diagram is used to show how outcomes of two events overlap.
  • Relative frequency (based on experimental results) becomes a better estimate of theoretical probability the more trials are performed.

Exam Paper Guide

Understand exactly what the Grade 10 Mathematics papers look like and how to maximise your marks.

📄 Paper Overview

  • Paper 1: 2 hours, 100 marks — Algebraic expressions, equations & inequalities, number patterns, functions & graphs, finance & growth.
  • Paper 2: 2 hours, 100 marks — Euclidean geometry, analytical geometry, trigonometry, statistics & probability.

🔑 Levels of Questions

  • Level 1 (Knowledge): direct recall — apply a formula exactly as given.
  • Level 2 (Routine procedures): well-known procedures done in a familiar context.
  • Level 3 (Complex procedures): problems requiring several steps or making connections between topics.
  • Level 4 (Problem solving): non-routine problems requiring insight and reasoning.

Mark Allocation Guide

Question typeWhat earns marks
Calculation/formula questionCorrect formula stated, correct substitution, and correct final answer.
Multi-step problemMethod marks are awarded at each step, even if the final answer is wrong.
Geometric proof/riderEvery statement needs a valid reason — an unsupported correct answer loses marks.
📌 Exam tip: Never leave a question blank — write down the relevant formula and substitute what you know; method marks are often available even without a final answer.

Formula & Calculation Guide

Every key Grade 10 Mathematics formula in one place, with a worked example for each.

1

Solving a Quadratic Equation by Factorisation

Set the equation equal to zero, factorise, apply the zero product rule.

Example: Solve x² + x − 6 = 0.
Factorise: (x+3)(x−2) = 0 → x = −3 or x = 2
2

General Term of a Linear Number Pattern

Tₙ = a + (n−1)d

Example: Sequence: 5, 8, 11, 14, ...
a=5, d=3. T₁₀ = 5 + (10−1)(3) = 5 + 27 = 32
3

Simple Interest

A = P(1 + i·n)

Example: R5,000 invested at 6% simple interest for 4 years.
A = 5,000 × (1 + 0.06×4) = 5,000 × 1.24 = R6,200
4

Compound Interest

A = P(1 + i)ⁿ

Example: R5,000 invested at 6% compound interest for 4 years.
A = 5,000 × (1.06)⁴ = 5,000 × 1.26247... = R6,312.38
5

Distance & Gradient (Analytical Geometry)

d = √((x₂−x₁)² + (y₂−y₁)²)
m = (y₂−y₁)/(x₂−x₁)

Example: A(1,2) and B(4,6).
d = √((4−1)² + (6−2)²) = √(9+16) = √25 = 5
m = (6−2)/(4−1) = 4/3
6

Basic Trig Ratio (SOH-CAH-TOA)

sinθ = Opp/Hyp, cosθ = Adj/Hyp, tanθ = Opp/Adj

Example: A right triangle has opposite side 6, hypotenuse 10.
sinθ = 6/10 = 0.6 → θ = 36.87°
