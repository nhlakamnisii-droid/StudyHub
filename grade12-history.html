Origins of the Cold War

The Cold War was a period of geopolitical tension between the United States (USA) and the Soviet Union (USSR) after World War II, primarily driven by ideological differences between capitalism/democracy and communism.

  • Yalta Conference (1945): Allied leaders divided post-war Europe — tensions emerged over Eastern Europe's future.
  • Truman Doctrine (1947): USA committed to containing communism globally; pledged aid to Greece and Turkey.
  • Marshall Plan (1948): USA provided $13 billion to rebuild Western Europe and prevent communist spread.
  • Berlin Blockade (1948–49): USSR blocked Western access to Berlin; USA responded with the Berlin Airlift.
🔑 Containment: US foreign policy of preventing the further spread of communism, articulated by George Kennan.

Key Events & Flashpoints

  • 1950–53 Korean War: First major Cold War armed conflict; Korea divided at 38th parallel.
  • 1957 Sputnik: USSR launched first satellite — sparked the Space Race.
  • 1961 Berlin Wall built to stop East Germans fleeing to the West.
  • 1962 Cuban Missile Crisis: USA and USSR on the brink of nuclear war; resolved diplomatically.

Cold War Alliances

  • NATO (1949): North Atlantic Treaty Organisation — Western capitalist bloc military alliance.
  • Warsaw Pact (1955): Soviet-led military alliance of Eastern communist states.

Détente — A Thawing of Tensions

In the 1970s, both superpowers pursued détente — a relaxation of hostilities — driven by the economic costs of the arms race and the recognition of nuclear parity.

  • SALT I (1972): Strategic Arms Limitation Talks — first treaty limiting nuclear weapons.
  • Helsinki Accords (1975): Recognised post-war European borders; included human rights provisions.
  • Nixon visits China (1972): USA opened diplomatic relations with communist China, exploiting Sino-Soviet split.
🔑 Détente: Policy of improving relations between the USA and USSR through diplomacy, trade, and arms control agreements.

End of the Cold War

  • Reagan Doctrine (1980s): USA actively supported anti-communist movements, ratcheting up pressure on USSR.
  • Gorbachev's Reforms: Glasnost (openness) and Perestroika (restructuring) weakened Soviet control.
  • 1989 Fall of the Berlin Wall: Symbol of communist collapse in Eastern Europe.
  • 1991 Dissolution of the USSR: 15 independent republics formed; Cold War ended.

The Civil Rights Movement (USA)

African Americans fought for racial equality through mass protest, legal challenges, and political pressure throughout the 1950s–60s.

  • 1954 Brown v. Board of Education: Supreme Court ruled racial segregation in schools unconstitutional.
  • 1955 Montgomery Bus Boycott: Rosa Parks refused to give up her seat; sparked 381-day boycott.
  • 1963 March on Washington: MLK delivered his "I Have a Dream" speech to 250,000 people.
  • 1964 Civil Rights Act: Banned discrimination on grounds of race, colour, religion, or national origin.
  • 1965 Voting Rights Act: Prohibited discriminatory voting practices targeting Black Americans.
🔑 Non-violent resistance: Strategy championed by MLK and influenced by Gandhi — used peaceful protest to expose injustice.

Vietnam War & Anti-War Protests

  • USA's involvement in Vietnam was driven by the domino theory — fear that communist Vietnam would lead to communist Southeast Asia.
  • Anti-war movement: Students, veterans, and civil rights activists protested against the war's cost and casualties.
  • Kent State Massacre (1970): National Guard shot and killed 4 student protesters — turned public opinion against the war.
  • My Lai Massacre (1968): US soldiers killed hundreds of civilians — undermined the war's moral justification.

Context: Apartheid South Africa

Apartheid (1948–1994) was a system of institutionalised racial segregation enforced by the National Party government.

  • Pass Laws, Group Areas Act, Population Registration Act: Key laws enforcing racial separation.
  • Sharpeville Massacre (1960): 69 protesters killed; ANC and PAC banned; resistance went underground.
  • Soweto Uprising (1976): Students protested Afrikaans-medium education; over 170 killed — reignited resistance.
🔑 UDF (1983): United Democratic Front — coalition of anti-apartheid organisations that coordinated internal resistance in the 1980s.

Transition to Democracy

  • 1990 F.W. de Klerk unbanned the ANC, PAC, and SACP; released Nelson Mandela after 27 years.
  • CODESA (1991–92): Convention for a Democratic South Africa — multiparty negotiations for a new constitution.
  • 1993 Interim Constitution agreed; Mandela and De Klerk jointly awarded Nobel Peace Prize.
  • 27 April 1994 First democratic elections: ANC won with 62.6%; Mandela became South Africa's first democratically elected president.
  • TRC (1996–98): Truth and Reconciliation Commission, chaired by Archbishop Desmond Tutu, investigated human rights violations.

African Independence Movements

The post-WWII era saw a wave of African decolonisation — by 1963, most of sub-Saharan Africa had gained independence from European powers.

  • Pan-Africanism: Movement promoting unity and solidarity among African people, popularised by Kwame Nkrumah.
  • Ghana (1957): First sub-Saharan African country to gain independence; Nkrumah became its first PM.
  • Organisation of African Unity (OAU, 1963): Promoted solidarity and coordination among African states.

The Congo Crisis (1960–1965)

  • The Congo gained independence from Belgium in 1960 under Patrice Lumumba as PM.
  • Katanga Secession: Moise Tshombe declared Katanga independent — Belgian mining interests backed this.
  • Lumumba's assassination (1961): CIA-backed plot; Lumumba killed — became a symbol of Cold War interference in Africa.
  • Mobutu Sese Seko seized power with US backing in 1965 — ruled for 32 years in authoritarian style.
🔑 Neo-colonialism: The continued economic and political influence of former colonial powers over newly independent African states.

What is Globalisation?

Globalisation refers to the increasing interdependence of economies, cultures, and societies across the world — accelerated after the Cold War by advances in technology, communication, and trade liberalisation.

  • WTO (1995): World Trade Organisation replaced GATT; governs international trade rules.
  • IMF & World Bank: Institutions providing loans to developing nations, often tied to structural adjustment conditions.
  • SAPs: Structural Adjustment Programmes imposed on African states — required privatisation, deregulation, and cuts to social spending.
🔑 Washington Consensus: Set of free-market economic policies promoted by the IMF and World Bank, often criticised for harming developing countries.

Effects on South Africa & Africa

  • Post-apartheid South Africa reintegrated into the global economy after sanctions lifted in 1994.
  • GEAR (Growth, Employment and Redistribution) policy adopted in 1996 — aligned with free-market principles but critics argued it failed to address inequality.
  • African states faced challenges: debt burdens, commodity dependence, and brain drain alongside opportunities in technology and trade.

Exam Paper Guide

Understand exactly what the NSC History papers look like and how to maximise your marks.

📄 Paper 1 — Overview

  • Duration: 3 hours
  • Total marks: 150
  • Topics: Cold War, Civil Society Protests
  • Sections: A (source-based) + B (essay)
  • Question 1–3: Section A — source analysis
  • Question 4–5: Section B — essay questions

📄 Paper 2 — Overview

  • Duration: 3 hours
  • Total marks: 150
  • Topics: Independent Africa, SA History, Globalisation
  • Sections: A (source-based) + B (essay)
  • Question 1–3: Section A — source analysis
  • Question 4–5: Section B — essay questions
⚠️ Important: You must answer ALL questions in Section A (source-based) and choose ONE essay from Section B per paper.

Section A — Source-Based Questions

Question TypeMarksStrategy
Comprehension (explain, describe)2–4 marksUse your own words; extract info directly from the source
Inference / Interpretation4–6 marksQuote from source + explain what it implies
Evaluating reliability / usefulness4–6 marksConsider author, date, purpose, bias, and limitations
Cross-referencing sources4–8 marksState how sources agree/differ + quote evidence from both
Contextualisation paragraph8 marksLink source to broader historical context with own knowledge

Key Source Evaluation Terms

Reliability

Ask: Is the source likely to give an accurate account? Consider the author's position, bias, and access to information. Use the acronym PADD — Purpose, Audience, Date, Details.

Usefulness

Ask: What does this source help us understand about the event or period? A biased source can still be useful — it shows us what one side believed.

Cross-referencing

Compare two or more sources. State whether they agree, disagree, or partially agree, and quote specific evidence from each to support your answer.

Contextualisation

Place the source in its broader historical context using your own knowledge. Do NOT just describe the source — explain what was happening at the time and why it matters.

⏱️ Time Management Tip: Spend roughly 90 minutes on Section A and 60 minutes on your essay. Leave 10 minutes at the end to review both.

Essay Writing Guide

The essay is worth 60 marks (40%) of each paper. Master this structure to score in the top bands.

📝 The History essay is marked on: Introduction (10) · Evidence/Argument (25) · Analysis (15) · Conclusion (10)
1

Unpack the Question

Before writing a single word, spend 5 minutes analysing the question.

  • Identify the command word: Discuss / Assess / Evaluate / To what extent / Explain why.
  • Identify the topic: What event, period, or theme are you writing about?
  • Identify the time period: What dates or era does the question cover?
  • Identify the argument direction: Is the question asking you to agree, disagree, or weigh up multiple factors?
2

Write a Strong Introduction (10 marks)

Your introduction should do three things:

  • Contextualise: Briefly describe the historical background (2–3 sentences).
  • Address the question: Directly respond to what is being asked.
  • State your argument: Your thesis — the overarching claim you will prove in the essay.
[Broad historical context — 2 sentences]. This essay will argue that [your thesis/main argument]. This will be demonstrated through an examination of [3 main points you will cover].
3

Develop Body Paragraphs (25 marks evidence + 15 marks analysis)

Each paragraph must follow the P.E.E.L structure:

  • P — Point: State your argument for this paragraph clearly.
  • E — Evidence: Provide specific facts, dates, names, events to support your point.
  • E — Explain: Explain how your evidence proves your point.
  • L — Link: Connect back to the question/thesis.
[Topic sentence — your paragraph argument]. This is supported by the fact that [specific evidence: name, date, event]. This demonstrates [your explanation/analysis], which shows that [link back to question].
  • Aim for 3–4 body paragraphs with different arguments or factors.
  • Include counter-arguments where relevant — then refute them for higher marks.
  • Use historical vocabulary: causation, consequence, continuity, change, significance, perspective.
4

Write a Strong Conclusion (10 marks)

Do NOT introduce new information. Your conclusion should:

  • Summarise your main arguments (1 sentence each).
  • Restate your thesis in light of the evidence discussed.
  • Make a final judgement: Answer the question directly and decisively.
In conclusion, this essay has argued that [restate thesis]. This was demonstrated through [summary of main points]. Therefore, it is clear that [final answer to the question].

Marking Rubric — What the Examiner Looks For

SectionMarksCriteria

Evidence 25 Accurate and relevant facts, dates, events, and names used to support arguments

TOTAL 60

Common Mistakes to Avoid

❌ Storytelling

Don't just narrate events. You must argue and analyse. Every paragraph should answer: So what? Why does this matter?

❌ Vague Statements

Avoid "many people suffered" — be specific: "Over 170 students were killed during the 1976 Soweto Uprising."

❌ Ignoring the Question

Re-read the question before every paragraph. Every point must connect back to what you are being asked.

❌ No Introduction/Conclusion

Many learners lose 20 marks simply by starting with a body paragraph. Always write a proper intro and conclusion.

✍️ Practice tip: Time yourself writing one essay in 60 minutes. Then swap with a classmate and mark each other using the rubric above.
