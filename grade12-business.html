📄 PAPER 1 — Business Environments & Operations
01
Business Environments
Paper 1 · Term 1
▾
The Three Business Environments
Every business operates within three interrelated environments that affect its decisions and performance.
Micro environment: internal environment the business controls — vision, mission, goals, management, employees, culture, resources
Market environment: factors the business can influence — customers, suppliers, competitors, intermediaries, financiers, trade unions
Macro environment: external factors the business CANNOT control — PESTLE factors
PESTLE — Macro Environment Factors
Political: government stability, elections, policy changes (e.g. land expropriation)
Economic: inflation, interest rates, GDP growth, unemployment, exchange rates
Social: demographics, culture, consumer trends, urbanisation, inequality
Technological: automation, e-commerce, AI, cybersecurity, load-shedding
Legal: legislation — LRA, BCEA, EEA, CPA, Companies Act
Environmental: climate change, sustainability, green business practices, water scarcity
SWOT Analysis
A strategic planning tool that evaluates internal and external factors affecting a business.
Strengths — internal positives the business controls (e.g. strong brand, skilled staff)
Weaknesses — internal negatives (e.g. high debt, poor management, outdated equipment)
Opportunities — external positives to exploit (e.g. new market, new technology)
Threats — external dangers (e.g. new competitors, recession, regulatory changes)
02
Business Legislation
Paper 1 · Term 1
▾
Labour Relations Act (LRA) — Act 66 of 1995
Regulates the relationship between employers and employees, and promotes collective bargaining.
Unfair dismissal: employees can dispute dismissals at the CCMA or Labour Court
Strikes: employees have the right to strike legally (protected strike) or illegally (unprotected)
CCMA: Commission for Conciliation, Mediation and Arbitration — resolves disputes
Collective bargaining: trade unions negotiate wages/conditions on behalf of workers
Basic Conditions of Employment Act (BCEA) — Act 75 of 1997
Working hours: max 45 hours/week; max 9 hours/day (5-day week) or 8 hours/day (6-day week)
Overtime: max 10 hours/week; paid at 1.5× normal rate
Leave: 21 consecutive days annual leave; 6 weeks sick leave per 3-year cycle; 4 months maternity leave
Notice periods: 1 week (under 6 months); 2 weeks (6m–1yr); 4 weeks (1yr+)
Employment Equity Act (EEA) — Act 55 of 1998
Promotes equal opportunity and fair treatment in the workplace, and implements affirmative action.
Designated groups: Black people, women, and people with disabilities
Affirmative action: measures to advance designated groups in the workplace
Employers must: report EE plans to the DoEL, eliminate unfair discrimination
Consumer Protection Act (CPA) — Act 68 of 2008
Protects consumers from unfair business practices and unsafe products.
Right to equality in the consumer market — no discrimination
Right to information — plain language contracts, full disclosure
Right to safety — products must not be hazardous
Right to return defective goods within 6 months for repair, replacement, or refund
Cooling-off period: 5 business days to cancel direct marketing contracts
03
Human Resources Function
Paper 1 · Term 2
▾
Recruitment & Selection
Recruitment = attracting applicants. Selection = choosing the best candidate.
Internal recruitment: promotion from within — cheaper, motivates staff, limited talent pool
External recruitment: advertising, agencies, social media — wider talent pool, higher cost
Selection process: shortlist → interviews → psychometric tests → reference checks → appointment
Employment contract: must include job title, duties, remuneration, hours, leave entitlement
Training & Development
Induction: orientation of new employees to the organisation
On-the-job training: coaching, mentoring, job rotation — done at the workplace
Off-the-job training: workshops, courses, simulations — done away from workplace
Benefits: improved productivity, reduced errors, better morale, legal compliance
Skills Development Act: requires businesses to contribute 1% of payroll to SETA learnerships
Remuneration & Motivation
Remuneration: total package — salary, wages, benefits, bonuses, allowances
Maslow's Hierarchy: Physiological → Safety → Social → Esteem → Self-actualisation
Herzberg Two-Factor: Hygiene factors (prevent dissatisfaction) vs Motivators (create satisfaction)
McGregor Theory X: workers dislike work, need control. Theory Y: workers are self-motivated
Dismissal & Termination
Fair dismissal: must be substantively fair (valid reason) AND procedurally fair (correct process followed)
Grounds for dismissal: misconduct, incapacity, operational requirements (retrenchment)
Disciplinary procedure: verbal warning → written warning → final warning → dismissal
Constructive dismissal: employer makes conditions intolerable, forcing employee to resign
04
Quality of Performance
Paper 1 · Term 3
▾
Total Quality Management (TQM)
TQM is a management philosophy focused on continuous improvement and customer satisfaction in all business processes.
Principles: customer focus, continuous improvement (Kaizen), employee involvement, process approach
Zero defects: doing things right the first time to eliminate waste
Quality circles: small groups of employees who meet regularly to identify and solve quality problems
ISO Standards
International Organisation for Standardisation — sets global quality standards for products, services, and systems.
ISO 9001: quality management systems — ensures consistent product/service quality
ISO 14001: environmental management — reduces environmental impact of operations
Benefits: improved credibility, access to global markets, reduced errors, higher customer trust
Quality Controls & Benchmarking
Quality control: inspection of finished products to detect defects before reaching customers
Quality assurance: proactive system ensuring quality is built in throughout the production process
Benchmarking: comparing your business performance against industry best practices or competitors
SABS: South African Bureau of Standards — certifies products meet SA quality and safety standards

Management & Leadership
Paper 2 · Term 2
▾
Management vs Leadership
Management: doing things right — planning, organising, controlling processes and resources
Leadership: doing the right things — inspiring, motivating, and guiding people toward a vision
Managers have formal authority. Leaders influence through personality and vision.
Leadership Styles
Autocratic: leader makes all decisions without consulting staff. Fast, but low morale and creativity.
Democratic: leader involves team in decisions. High morale and buy-in, but slower decision-making.
Laissez-faire: leader delegates all decision-making to employees. Works for highly skilled, self-motivated teams.
Transactional: rewards/punishes based on performance — exchange relationship
Transformational: inspires employees to exceed expectations through vision and passion
Conflict Management
Conflict is a disagreement arising from differences in values, goals, or interests. It can be constructive (leads to improvement) or destructive.
Avoidance: ignoring the conflict — short-term fix, problem remains
Compromise: both parties give up something — middle-ground solution
Collaboration: both parties work together to find a win-win solution — best approach
Accommodation: one party gives in to the other — keeps peace but may breed resentment
Forcing: one party imposes their solution — quick but damages relationships
06
Investment & Insurance
Paper 2 · Term 2
▾
Securities (Shares)
A share represents a unit of ownership in a company. Shares are bought and sold on the JSE (Johannesburg Stock Exchange).
Ordinary shares: voting rights, variable dividends, last claim on assets if company liquidates
Preference shares: fixed dividends paid first, no voting rights, priority over ordinary shares on liquidation
JSE: regulates buying/selling of shares; provides liquidity and price discovery
Bull market: share prices rising, investor confidence high
Bear market: share prices falling, investors pessimistic
Interest Calculations
Simple interest: I = P × r × t (interest on original principal only)
Compound interest: A = P(1 + r)ⁿ (interest earned on interest — grows faster)
Example: R10,000 at 10% for 3 years: Simple = R3,000 interest. Compound = R13,310 total (R3,310 interest).
Insurance
Insurance transfers risk from an individual/business to an insurer in exchange for premium payments.
Compulsory insurance: required by law — e.g. UIF (unemployment), compensation for occupational injuries (COIDA), Road Accident Fund
Non-compulsory insurance: optional — e.g. fire, theft, life, vehicle, business interruption
Insurable interest: the insured must have a financial stake in what is insured
Indemnity: insurance restores you to your position before the loss — you cannot profit from a claim
07
Ethics & Professionalism
Paper 2 · Term 3
▾
Business Ethics
Ethics refers to the moral principles that guide the behaviour of individuals and businesses in society.
Ethical behaviour: honesty, fairness, transparency, respect, accountability
Unethical behaviour: fraud, bribery, corruption, false advertising, exploitation, price-fixing (cartel)
Code of ethics: a written document outlining expected ethical standards in an organisation
Whistleblowing: reporting unethical behaviour — protected by the Protected Disclosures Act in SA
Professionalism
Conducting yourself with competence, integrity, and respect in the workplace
Attributes: punctuality, appropriate dress, effective communication, continuous learning, confidentiality
Professional image: how a business presents itself — branding, customer service, employee conduct
King IV Report: SA's governance framework promoting ethical leadership, accountability, and transparency in business
Creative Thinking & Problem Solving
Creative thinking: generating new, original ideas — divergent thinking, thinking outside the box
Critical thinking: evaluating ideas logically and objectively — convergent thinking
SWOT in problem solving: identify strengths to leverage, weaknesses to address, opportunities to exploit, threats to mitigate
SCAMPER technique: Substitute, Combine, Adapt, Modify, Put to other uses, Eliminate, Reverse
Mind mapping: visual brainstorming tool to generate and organise ideas
08
Corporate Social Responsibility (CSR)
Paper 2 · Term 3
▾
CSR vs CSI
CSR (Corporate Social Responsibility): the ongoing ethical obligation of a business to act in the best interests of society, the environment, and its stakeholders.
CSI (Corporate Social Investment): a specific component of CSR — voluntary financial or in-kind contributions to community upliftment projects.
CSI often involves donating to education, health, sports, arts — e.g. Shoprite Feeding Schemes, Vodacom's Literacy Programme
Under B-BBEE codes, CSI counts toward a company's transformation scorecard
Triple Bottom Line (3 Ps)
Businesses should be accountable across three dimensions, not just profit.
People: fair treatment of employees, communities, and customers
Planet: environmental sustainability — reducing carbon footprint, waste management
Profit: financial sustainability — remaining viable long term
Human Rights, Inclusivity & Environmental Issues
Constitution (Section 9): right to equality — businesses may not discriminate
Inclusivity: accommodating people with disabilities, gender diversity, cultural diversity in the workplace
Environmental issues: carbon emissions, waste disposal, water usage — businesses must comply with NEMA (National Environmental Management Act)
Green business: renewable energy use, recycling programmes, sustainable packaging

Business Ventures & Entrepreneurship
Paper 2 · Term 1
▾
Forms of Ownership
Sole trader: one owner, unlimited liability, easy to start, all profit kept
Partnership: 2–20 partners, shared liability, governed by Partnership Act
Private company (Pty) Ltd: 1–50 shareholders, limited liability, not listed on JSE
Public company (Ltd): unlimited shareholders, listed on JSE, must publish financials
Cooperative: owned and run by members for mutual benefit — e.g. stokvels, farming coops
Business Plan Components
Executive summary: brief overview of the entire plan
Business description: what the business does, vision, mission, goals
Market analysis: target market, competitors, SWOT
Marketing plan: 4 Ps — Product, Price, Place, Promotion
Financial plan: start-up costs, projected income statement, cash flow forecast
Operational plan: day-to-day running, suppliers, location, equipment

Exam Tips
Section C Essay Writing
Use subheadings — they earn format marks. State the definition of key terms first. Write at least 15 body points (each = 2 marks). End with a mini conclusion that summarises your argument.
Scenario Questions (Section B)
Always quote from the scenario. Link the extract to theory explicitly. Don't just define — explain HOW it applies to the scenario. Use signal words: "As illustrated in the scenario…", "This is an example of…"
Legislation Questions
Always state the full name AND the act number — e.g. "Labour Relations Act (LRA), Act 66 of 1995." Examiners reward precision. Know the differences between LRA, BCEA, EEA, and CPA.
Time Management
Section A: 30 min. Section B: 60 min (3 questions × 20 min each). Section C: 30 min. Always attempt Section C — 5 format marks are free if you write a heading and intro.
