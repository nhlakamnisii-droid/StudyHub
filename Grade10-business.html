The Three Business Environments

  • Micro environment: factors the business has direct control over — its mission, resources, and internal operations.
  • Market environment: factors closely linked to the business but only partly under its control — customers, competitors, suppliers.
  • Macro environment: factors completely outside the business's control — political, economic, social, technological, legal, and environmental (PESTLE) factors.
🔑 Why this matters: a business must constantly scan all three environments to identify opportunities and threats and adapt its strategy accordingly.

The Three Sectors of the Economy

  • Primary sector: extracts raw materials directly from nature (e.g. farming, mining, fishing).
  • Secondary sector: manufactures/processes raw materials into finished products (e.g. factories, construction).
  • Tertiary sector: provides services rather than physical goods (e.g. banking, retail, tourism, education).

A single supply chain often moves through all three sectors — e.g. cotton is farmed (primary), woven into fabric and sewn into clothing (secondary), then sold in a shop (tertiary).

Common Forms of Ownership

  • Sole trader: owned and run by one person; unlimited liability; easy and cheap to start.
  • Partnership: owned by 2–20 partners who share profits, decisions, and unlimited liability.
  • Private company (Pty Ltd): owned by shareholders; limited liability; shares are not sold to the public.
  • Public company (Ltd): shares are listed and sold on a stock exchange; limited liability.
  • Co-operative: owned and controlled by its members for their mutual benefit, each member typically has one vote regardless of investment.
🔑 Limited liability: owners are only liable for business debts up to the amount they invested — their personal assets are protected. Unlimited liability means personal assets can be used to pay business debts.

The Four Core Management Functions

  • Planning: setting goals and deciding the best way to achieve them.
  • Organising: arranging resources (people, money, equipment) to carry out the plan.
  • Leading: directing, motivating, and communicating with employees to achieve goals.
  • Control: monitoring performance against the plan and taking corrective action where needed.
🔑 These four functions are cyclical: control feeds back into planning, so managers can continually refine their approach.

Key Entrepreneurial Qualities

  • Risk-taking, self-confidence, perseverance, innovation, self-discipline, and the ability to spot opportunities others miss.

Creative Thinking & Problem Solving

Entrepreneurs use creative thinking to generate original solutions to problems and identify gaps in the market that others have overlooked.

🔑 Innovation is putting a creative idea into practice in a way that adds value — an idea alone is not innovation until it is actually implemented.

Purpose of a Business Plan

A written document that describes the business idea, how it will operate, and how it will make a profit — used to guide the owner and to convince investors/banks to provide funding.

Key Sections of a Business Plan

  • Executive summary: a brief overview of the whole plan.
  • Business description: what the business does and its mission/vision.
  • Marketing plan: target market, competitors, and marketing strategy.
  • Operational plan: day-to-day running of the business.
  • Financial plan: start-up costs, funding needed, and projected income/expenses.

What Is Business Ethics?

The moral principles that guide how a business behaves towards its employees, customers, suppliers, and the wider community.

Examples of Ethical Business Behaviour

  • Paying fair wages and providing safe working conditions.
  • Being honest in advertising and not misleading customers.
  • Protecting the environment and reducing waste/pollution.
  • Paying taxes honestly and on time.
🔑 Corporate social responsibility (CSR): a business's obligation to act in ways that benefit society, beyond just making a profit.

SWOT Analysis

Strengths & Weaknesses — internal factors
Opportunities & Threats — external factors
  • Strengths: what the business does well or has an advantage in.
  • Weaknesses: internal limitations that put the business at a disadvantage.
  • Opportunities: external conditions the business could exploit for growth.
  • Threats: external conditions that could harm the business.
🔑 Identifying business opportunities: successful entrepreneurs look for unmet needs, gaps in the market, or problems worth solving — then match a viable business idea to that gap.

Exam Paper Guide

Understand exactly what the Grade 10 Business Studies paper looks like and how to maximise your marks.

📄 Paper Overview

  • Typically one paper of around 2–2.5 hours, made up of short questions, a case-study-based section, and an essay/structured question.
  • Content is usually drawn from business environments, sectors, ownership, management, entrepreneurship, and ethics.

🔑 Levels of Questions

  • Level 1 (Knowledge): defining a term or naming a concept.
  • Level 2 (Comprehension): explaining or describing a concept in context.
  • Level 3 (Application): applying theory to a given business scenario.
  • Level 4 (Analysis/Evaluation): comparing options or recommending a course of action.

Mark Allocation Guide

Question typeWhat earns marks
Definition questionThe exact, complete definition using correct business terminology.
List/name questionOne clearly distinct point per mark — no overlapping or repeated ideas.
Case-study application questionTheory correctly linked to specific evidence from the given scenario.
📌 Exam tip: Match your answer length to the marks available — a 4-mark question needs roughly four distinct, well-explained points.

Case Study Answering Technique

Business Studies questions are almost always built around a short business scenario — here's how to answer them for full marks.

1

Read the Case Study Twice

First read for the overall story — what business is this, what's the situation? Second read, underline names, numbers, and any specific problem mentioned.

2

Identify the Command Word & Marks

Check whether the question asks you to name, explain, analyse, or recommend — and how many marks it's worth. This tells you how much detail to give.

3

Apply Theory to the Scenario — Don't Write Generically

A generic textbook answer earns few marks. You must link your point directly to the business in the case study.

[Business in the case study] shows [theory/concept] because [specific evidence from the case study]. This means [effect/consequence for the business].
4

Use Correct Business Terminology

Examiners look for the exact CAPS terms — e.g. "macro environment" not "outside stuff," "limited liability" not "safe from debt."

Marking Rubric — What the Examiner Looks For

CriteriaWhat earns the mark
RelevanceAnswer is directly linked to the case study, not generic theory.
TerminologyCorrect CAPS business vocabulary is used.
ApplicationTheory is applied to the specific scenario given, with evidence quoted from the text.
StructureAnswer matches the mark allocation — neither too short nor padded.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

❌ Generic Theory Only

Don't just recite a definition. Always connect it back to the business named in the case study.

❌ Ignoring Mark Allocation

A 2-mark question needs two points — one-word answers or single facts lose marks.

❌ Wrong Terminology

"The business has no money" should read "the business has a liquidity problem."

❌ Not Reading Carefully

Many learners miss key details (numbers, names, dates) given in the case study that the question depends on.

🧩 Practice tip: After studying a topic, write your own 2-line mini case study and answer one question on it — this builds the "apply, don't recite" habit examiners reward.
