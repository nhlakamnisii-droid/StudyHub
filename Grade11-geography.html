Mid-Latitude Cyclones

  • Form along the polar front, where cold polar air meets warm subtropical air.
  • Have a cold front (steep, fast-moving, brings heavy short bursts of rain) and a warm front (gentler slope, brings prolonged lighter rain).
  • Associated with the jet stream, which steers the system and intensifies it.
  • Bring cloudy, rainy, windy weather to the southern and south-western Cape in winter.
🔑 Occlusion: the point where a faster-moving cold front catches up to and merges with a warm front, lifting the warm air completely off the ground.

Tropical Cyclones

  • Form over warm ocean water (≥26.5°C) near, but not on, the equator (needs Coriolis force to spin).
  • Structure: a calm eye at the centre, surrounded by the eye wall (strongest winds and rain), with spiralling rain bands.
  • Cause damage through strong winds, storm surges, and flooding — affect Mozambique and northern KwaZulu-Natal.
🔑 Coriolis force: the deflection of moving air caused by the Earth's rotation — essential for tropical cyclones to form and spin.

River Processes

  • Erosion: hydraulic action, abrasion, attrition, and solution wear away the riverbed and banks.
  • Transportation: traction, saltation, suspension, and solution carry sediment downstream.
  • Deposition: occurs when a river loses energy/velocity and drops its sediment load.

River Landforms

  • Upper course: V-shaped valleys, waterfalls, rapids — dominated by vertical erosion.
  • Middle course: meanders form as lateral erosion widens the valley.
  • Lower course: floodplains, levees, oxbow lakes, and deltas form as deposition dominates.
🔑 Oxbow lake: a curved lake formed when a meander is cut off from the main river channel.

Drainage Systems

  • Drainage basin: the area of land drained by a river and its tributaries, bounded by a watershed.
  • Drainage patterns: dendritic (tree-like, on uniform rock), trellis (rectangular, on alternating hard/soft rock), radial (flows outward from a central high point, e.g. a dome or volcano).

Rural Settlement Patterns

  • Dispersed/scattered: houses spread far apart, common in commercial farming areas.
  • Nucleated/clustered: houses grouped tightly together, often around a shared resource like water or a road.
  • Linear: settlements strung out along a road, river, or coastline.
🔑 Site: the actual physical land a settlement is built on. Situation: the location of a settlement relative to its surroundings (other towns, resources, routes).

Rural Land Use & Challenges

  • Rural land use includes subsistence and commercial farming, forestry, mining, and conservation areas.
  • Challenges: poverty, poor infrastructure and service delivery, land degradation, and rural-to-urban migration ("push factors").
  • Rural development strategies: improving access to water, roads, schools, and clinics; supporting land reform and agricultural extension programmes.

Urban Structure & Land Use Models

  • The CBD (Central Business District) is the commercial core of a city, with the highest land values and greatest accessibility.
  • Bid-rent theory: land value and rent decrease with distance from the CBD, explaining why the tallest, densest buildings cluster in the centre.
  • Urban land use zones typically include the CBD, industrial areas, and residential zones of varying density.
🔑 Urbanisation: the increasing proportion of a country's population living in urban (town/city) areas.

Urban Challenges

  • Informal settlements: unplanned housing areas that develop due to rapid urbanisation and housing shortages, often lacking basic services.
  • Urban sprawl: the uncontrolled outward expansion of a city into surrounding rural land.
  • Traffic congestion, pollution, and unemployment are common challenges in rapidly growing cities.
  • South African cities show a legacy of apartheid spatial planning, with historically segregated townships often located far from economic opportunities.

Economic Sectors

  • Primary extraction of raw materials — mining, farming, fishing, forestry.
  • Secondary manufacturing and processing of raw materials into finished products.
  • Tertiary the provision of services — retail, banking, tourism, transport.
  • Quaternary knowledge-based industries — research, IT, and information services.

South Africa's Economy

  • Mining (especially gold, platinum, and coal) has historically driven South Africa's industrial development.
  • The tertiary sector now employs the largest share of South Africa's workforce.
  • Informal sector: unregistered, untaxed economic activity (e.g. street trading) that provides income where formal jobs are scarce.
🔑 Economic development: improvement in a region's economy, alongside gains in living standards, infrastructure, and opportunity — not just growth in output.

Development & Sustainability

  • Regional inequalities exist between South Africa's provinces, and between urban and rural areas.
  • Sustainable development balances economic growth with social equity and environmental protection for future generations.

Exam Paper Guide

Understand exactly what the Grade 11 Geography papers look like and how to maximise your marks.

📄 Paper Overview

  • Paper 1 (Theory): 2 hours, 150 marks — climate/weather, geomorphology, rural & urban settlements, economic geography.
  • Paper 2 (Map Work): 1.5 hours, 75 marks — topographic map and orthophoto map skills, plus GIS.

🔑 Command Words

  • Name/Identify/List: 1 mark per correct fact.
  • Describe: give the characteristics or features of something.
  • Explain/Account for: give reasons and processes — the "why," not just the "what."
  • Discuss/Evaluate: weigh up different sides of an issue, showing insight.

Mark Allocation Guide

Question typeMarks per pointWhat's expected
Name/Identify1 markA single correct term or fact.
Describe1–2 marksA clear characteristic or feature, correctly stated.
Explain/Account for2 marksA cause-and-effect point showing understanding of process.
Map calculation (scale, gradient, etc.)Full marks for method + answerCorrect formula, substitution, and final answer with units.
📌 Exam tip: Always give the correct geographic term first, then explain it in your own words — examiners reward precise vocabulary like "occlusion," "bid-rent," or "dendritic drainage."

Map Work & Data Handling Guide

Core topographic map and GIS skills tested in Geography Paper 2, with worked examples.

1

Scale & Distance Calculations

Actual distance = Map distance × Scale

Example: On a 1:50,000 map, two points are 6 cm apart.
Actual distance = 6 cm × 50,000 = 300,000 cm = 3 km
2

Gradient Calculation

Gradient = Vertical Interval (VI) ÷ Horizontal Equivalent (HE)

Example: VI = 100 m, HE = 2,000 m.
Gradient = 100 ÷ 2,000 = 1:20 (expressed as a ratio)
3

Grid References

Give the four-figure or six-figure grid reference by reading eastings first, then northings (right, then up) — remember the phrase "along the corridor, up the stairs."

4

Contour Lines & Relief

  • Closely spaced contours = steep slope; widely spaced contours = gentle slope.
  • V-shaped contours pointing uphill (away from higher ground) indicate a river valley.
  • Concentric circles of contours indicate a hill or depression — check the value of the innermost contour to tell them apart.
5

GIS (Geographic Information Systems) Basics

🔑 GIS: a computer-based system that captures, stores, analyses, and displays geographically referenced (spatial) data in layers.
  • Layers: different types of spatial data (roads, rivers, land use) stacked to build a composite map.
  • Vector data: represents features as points, lines, and polygons.
  • Raster data: represents data as a grid of cells/pixels, e.g. satellite imagery.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

❌ Wrong Units

Forgetting to convert cm to km, or leaving out units altogether — always state the final unit clearly.

❌ Reversing Grid References

Giving northings before eastings is one of the most common map work errors — eastings always come first.

❌ No Working Shown

Calculation questions award marks for method — always show your formula and substitution, not just the final answer.

❌ Misreading Contour Values

Always check the contour interval given in the map key before interpreting slope steepness or landform shape.

🗺️ Practice tip: Practise on a real topographic map extract every week — reading real contour patterns builds the pattern-recognition skill that no amount of theory alone can teach.
