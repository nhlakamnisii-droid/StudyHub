Ethics in Accounting

  • Ethical behaviour means being honest, transparent, and accountable when handling financial information.
  • Professional bodies such as SAICA (South African Institute of Chartered Accountants) and SAIPA set standards of conduct for the profession.

Internal Control

🔑 Internal control: the systems and procedures a business puts in place to safeguard assets and ensure accurate financial records.
  • Division of duties: no single person should control an entire transaction from start to finish, reducing fraud risk.
  • Documentation: every transaction should have supporting source documents (invoices, receipts).
  • Physical controls: e.g. safes, locked storerooms, access control.
  • Internal audit: an ongoing independent review of a business's financial processes.

Bank Reconciliation

Comparing the business's Cash Journal balance to the bank statement balance to explain any differences.

  • Outstanding deposits: recorded in the business's books but not yet reflected on the bank statement.
  • Outstanding cheques: issued but not yet presented for payment.
  • Bank charges/debit orders: appear on the statement before being recorded in the Cash Journal.
  • Dishonoured cheques: cheques the bank refuses to pay (e.g. insufficient funds).

Creditors Reconciliation

  • Compares the Creditors' Ledger Account (business's records) to the Creditor's Statement (supplier's records).
  • Differences usually come from timing (goods/payments in transit) or errors on either side.

Types of Fixed Assets

  • Land & buildings, vehicles, equipment — resources a business owns and uses long-term to generate income.

Depreciation Methods

🔑 Depreciation: the systematic allocation of an asset's cost as an expense over its useful life.
  • Straight-line method (cost method): the same depreciation amount is charged every year.
  • Diminishing balance method (reducing balance): depreciation is calculated on the asset's carrying value, so the amount decreases each year.
Straight-line = Cost × rate%
Diminishing balance = Carrying value × rate%

Asset Disposal

  • When an asset is sold, compare the selling price to its carrying value (cost − accumulated depreciation) to determine a profit or loss on sale.
  • A Fixed Asset Register/Note tracks each asset's cost, accumulated depreciation, and carrying value.

What Is a Partnership?

An agreement between 2 or more people who carry on a business together with the aim of making and sharing profit.

  • Governed by a partnership agreement setting out capital contributions, profit-sharing ratios, and partner responsibilities.

Capital vs Current Accounts

  • Capital account: records each partner's fixed contribution — stays constant unless partners agree to change it.
  • Current account: records a partner's share of profit, salary, interest on capital, and drawings — fluctuates regularly.

Appropriation Account

Shows how net profit is distributed among partners:

  • Partners' salaries (if agreed).
  • Interest on capital (rewards partners for the capital they've invested).
  • Bonus to a partner (if agreed).
  • Remaining profit shared in the agreed profit-sharing ratio.
Interest on capital = Capital balance × rate% × time

Why Analyse Financial Statements?

Ratios help owners, investors, and creditors assess a business's liquidity, profitability, and solvency.

Key Ratios

Current ratio = Current assets : Current liabilities
Acid-test ratio = (Current assets − Inventory) : Current liabilities
Gross profit margin = (Gross profit ÷ Sales) × 100
Net profit margin = (Net profit ÷ Sales) × 100
Solvency ratio = Total assets : Total liabilities
  • Liquidity ratios (current, acid-test): can the business pay its short-term debts?
  • Profitability ratios (gross/net profit margin): how well does the business turn sales into profit?
  • Solvency ratio: can the business cover all its liabilities if it had to close down?
🔑 Liquidity: a business's ability to pay its short-term debts as they fall due.

Cost Accounting

  • Direct costs: direct material + direct labour — costs traceable directly to a product.
  • Indirect costs (factory overheads): costs that support production but aren't traceable to one product, e.g. factory rent.
  • Break-even point: the level of sales at which a business makes neither profit nor loss.
Break-even point (units) = Fixed costs ÷ (Selling price per unit − Variable cost per unit)

Budgeting

  • Cash Budget: projects expected cash inflows and outflows to help manage liquidity.
  • Projected Income Statement: forecasts expected income and expenses for a future period.

Inventory Systems

  • Perpetual system: inventory records are updated continuously with every purchase and sale.
  • Periodic system: inventory is only counted and valued at the end of a period.
  • Valuation methods: FIFO (First-In-First-Out) and weighted average cost.

What Is VAT?

🔑 VAT: an indirect tax charged on the value added to goods and services at each stage of production and distribution — currently 15% in South Africa.

Input vs Output VAT

  • Input VAT: VAT paid on purchases — can be claimed back from SARS.
  • Output VAT: VAT charged on sales — owed to SARS.
VAT payable to SARS = Output VAT − Input VAT
  • If Input VAT exceeds Output VAT, the business receives a VAT refund instead.
  • Tracked through the VAT Control Account.

Ethics & VAT

  • VAT fraud (e.g. claiming input VAT without valid tax invoices) is illegal and unethical.
  • Internal controls should govern the collection of VAT from customers and payment of VAT to SARS.

Exam Paper Guide

How to approach the Grade 11 Accounting paper and protect your marks.

📄 Paper Format

  • Structured questions covering each topic — reconciliations, assets, partnerships, ratios, cost accounting, VAT.
  • Marks are awarded for correct figures AND for showing your workings (method marks).
  • Always check the mark allocation — it tells you how many steps/figures are expected.

✅ Presentation Rules

  • Use the R sign only where required by the specific format (check the question).
  • Show ALL workings in brackets — most marks are for method, not just the final answer.
  • Round off to 2 decimal places unless told otherwise.
  • Underline final totals.

Mark Allocation Guide

Question typeWhat earns marks
Ledger accounts / journalsCorrect account names, amounts, and folio references.
Calculations (ratios, depreciation, VAT)Correct formula applied + correct final figure — workings shown for part marks.
Interpretation questionsA trend/figure identified PLUS a reason or comment — one alone rarely earns full marks.

Formula & Calculation Guide

Every key Grade 11 Accounting formula in one place, with a worked example for each.

1

Depreciation — Straight-Line Method

Depreciation = Cost × rate%

Example: Equipment cost R40,000, depreciated at 10% p.a. straight-line.
Depreciation = R40,000 × 10% = R4,000 per year
2

Depreciation — Diminishing Balance Method

Depreciation = Carrying value × rate%

Example: Vehicle carrying value R60,000, depreciated at 20% p.a. diminishing balance.
Depreciation = R60,000 × 20% = R12,000 in Year 1
3

Liquidity Ratios

Current ratio = Current assets : Current liabilities
Acid-test ratio = (Current assets − Inventory) : Current liabilities

Example: Current assets R150,000, Inventory R50,000, Current liabilities R75,000.
Current ratio = 150,000 : 75,000 = 2 : 1
Acid-test = (150,000 − 50,000) : 75,000 = 1.3 : 1
4

Break-Even Point

Break-even (units) = Fixed costs ÷ (Selling price − Variable cost per unit)

Example: Fixed costs R20,000, Selling price R50/unit, Variable cost R30/unit.
Break-even = 20,000 ÷ (50 − 30) = 1,000 units
5

VAT Payable to SARS

VAT payable = Output VAT − Input VAT

Example: Output VAT R15,000, Input VAT R9,000.
VAT payable = 15,000 − 9,000 = R6,000 owed to SARS
