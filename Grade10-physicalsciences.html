Scalars vs Vectors

  • Scalar: a quantity with magnitude only — e.g. distance, speed, mass, time.
  • Vector: a quantity with both magnitude AND direction — e.g. displacement, velocity, acceleration, force.

Distance vs Displacement

🔑 Distance is the total path length travelled (scalar); displacement is the straight-line distance from start to end point, with direction (vector).

Speed vs Velocity

  • Speed = distance ÷ time (scalar).
  • Velocity = displacement ÷ time (vector) — always include a direction in your answer.

Vector Diagrams

  • Vectors are drawn as arrows: the length represents magnitude and the arrowhead shows direction.
  • Vectors in the same direction are added directly; vectors in opposite directions are subtracted.

Motion Graphs

  • Position-time graph: the gradient (slope) at any point gives the velocity at that instant.
  • Velocity-time graph: the gradient gives acceleration; the area under the graph gives displacement.

Uniform Motion

  • Uniform velocity produces a straight diagonal line on a position-time graph.
  • Uniform acceleration produces a straight diagonal line on a velocity-time graph.

Acceleration

a = Δv / Δt

Acceleration is the rate of change of velocity — how quickly velocity is changing per second.

Instantaneous vs Average

🔑 Instantaneous velocity/speed is the value at one specific moment (the tangent gradient); average velocity/speed is the total change divided by the total time.

Describing Waves

v = fλ   (T = 1/f)
  • Wavelength (λ): distance between two successive identical points on a wave.
  • Frequency (f): number of waves passing a point per second; period (T) is the time for one complete wave.
  • Amplitude: the maximum displacement from the equilibrium (rest) position.

Transverse vs Longitudinal Waves

  • Transverse: particles vibrate perpendicular to the direction of wave travel (e.g. light, water waves).
  • Longitudinal: particles vibrate parallel to the direction of wave travel (e.g. sound).

Sound & Light

  • Sound is a longitudinal wave and needs a medium to travel through — it cannot travel through a vacuum.
  • Pitch depends on frequency; loudness depends on amplitude.
  • Reflection: angle of incidence = angle of reflection. Refraction: light bends as it passes from one medium into another of different density.

Electrostatics

  • There are two types of charge — positive and negative. Like charges repel; unlike charges attract.
  • Charge is always conserved — it is never created or destroyed, only transferred.
Coulomb's Law: F = kQ₁Q₂ / r²

Electric Circuits

  • Current (I): rate of flow of charge, measured in amperes (A).
  • Potential difference (V): the "push" driving current through a component, measured in volts (V).
  • Resistance (R): opposition to current flow, measured in ohms (Ω).
Ohm's Law: V = IR    Power: P = VI
🔑 Series circuits: same current throughout, voltage divides across components. Parallel circuits: same voltage across each branch, current divides.

Classifying Matter

  • Pure substances: elements (one type of atom) and compounds (two or more elements chemically combined).
  • Mixtures: homogeneous (uniform composition, e.g. saltwater) or heterogeneous (visibly different parts, e.g. sand and water).

States of Matter & the Kinetic Molecular Theory

  • Solid: particles closely packed, fixed positions, vibrate only.
  • Liquid: particles close together but can move past one another.
  • Gas: particles far apart, moving freely and rapidly in all directions.

Physical vs Chemical Change

🔑 Physical change: no new substance is formed (e.g. melting, boiling). Chemical change: a new substance is formed, often with a colour change, gas release, or temperature change.

Structure of the Atom

  • Protons: positively charged, found in the nucleus.
  • Neutrons: no charge (neutral), found in the nucleus.
  • Electrons: negatively charged, found in energy levels (shells) around the nucleus.
Atomic number (Z) = number of protons
Mass number (A) = protons + neutrons

The Periodic Table

  • Elements are arranged in order of increasing atomic number.
  • Groups (columns) contain elements with similar chemical properties and the same number of valence electrons.
  • Periods (rows) show an increasing number of occupied energy levels (shells).
🔑 Metals are found on the left of the periodic table, non-metals on the right, with metalloids along the zig-zag line between them.

Ionic Bonding

Formed by the transfer of electrons from a metal to a non-metal, producing oppositely charged ions (a positive cation and a negative anion) held together by electrostatic attraction.

Covalent Bonding

Formed by the sharing of electrons between two non-metal atoms — can be a single, double, or triple bond depending on how many electron pairs are shared.

Metallic Bonding

🔑 Metallic bonding: metal atoms form a lattice of positive ions surrounded by a "sea" of delocalised electrons — this explains why metals conduct electricity and can be bent without breaking.

Electronegativity

A measure of how strongly an atom attracts shared electrons in a bond — the bigger the difference in electronegativity between two atoms, the more polar (or ionic) the bond becomes.

Balancing Chemical Equations

🔑 Conservation of mass: matter cannot be created or destroyed in a reaction — the same number of atoms of each element must appear on both sides of a balanced equation.

The Mole Concept

n = m / M
  • n = number of moles, m = mass (g), M = molar mass (g/mol).
  • Avogadro's number: 1 mole of any substance contains 6.022 × 10²³ particles.

Types of Reactions

  • Synthesis: two or more substances combine to form one product.
  • Decomposition: one substance breaks down into two or more simpler substances.
  • Combustion: a substance reacts rapidly with oxygen, releasing energy.

Reactions in Aqueous Solution

Acids and bases react together in a neutralisation reaction to produce a salt and water.

Exam Paper Guide

Understand exactly what the Grade 10 Physical Sciences papers look like and how to maximise your marks.

📄 Paper Overview

  • Paper 1 (Physics): typically 2 hours — vectors & scalars, motion, waves sound & light, electrostatics & circuits.
  • Paper 2 (Chemistry): typically 2 hours — matter & classification, atomic structure, chemical bonding, chemical reactions & the mole concept.

🔑 Levels of Questions

  • Level 1 (Recall): stating a definition, law, or fact exactly.
  • Level 2 (Comprehension): explaining a concept in your own words, or a simple one-step calculation.
  • Level 3 (Application): multi-step calculations or applying a concept to a new context.
  • Level 4 (Analysis/Synthesis): interpreting graphs or data and drawing conclusions.

Mark Allocation Guide

Question typeWhat earns marks
Definition questionThe exact, complete definition — missing one key word can lose the mark.
Calculation questionCorrect formula, correct substitution with units, and correct final answer with units.
Graph/data interpretationCorrectly reading values off a graph or table and explaining what the trend means.
📌 Exam tip: Always include units in your final answer — a numerically correct answer without units often loses a mark.

Formula & Calculation Guide

Every key Grade 10 Physical Sciences formula in one place, with a worked example for each.

1

Average Velocity

v = Δx / Δt

Example: A car travels 150 m east in 10 s.
v = 150 / 10 = 15 m·s⁻¹ east
2

Wave Speed

v = fλ

Example: A wave has frequency 20 Hz and wavelength 0.5 m.
v = 20 × 0.5 = 10 m·s⁻¹
3

Coulomb's Law

F = kQ₁Q₂ / r²   (k = 9×10⁹ N·m²·C⁻²)

Example: Two charges of 2×10⁻⁶ C are 0.1 m apart.
F = (9×10⁹ × 2×10⁻⁶ × 2×10⁻⁶) / (0.1)² = 3.6 N
4

Ohm's Law

V = IR

Example: A resistor has current 2 A and resistance 5 Ω.
V = 2 × 5 = 10 V
5

Electrical Power

P = VI

Example: A device operates at 12 V and draws 3 A.
P = 12 × 3 = 36 W
6

The Mole Concept

n = m / M

Example: Find the number of moles in 36 g of water (M=18 g/mol).
n = 36 / 18 = 2 mol
