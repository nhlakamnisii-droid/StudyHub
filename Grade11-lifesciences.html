Study Notes

Biological Macromolecules

  • Carbohydrates: made of C, H, O; main energy source; includes monosaccharides (glucose), disaccharides (sucrose), and polysaccharides (starch, cellulose, glycogen).
  • Lipids: fats and oils; made of glycerol and fatty acids; used for long-term energy storage, insulation, and membrane structure.
  • Proteins: made of amino acid chains; function as enzymes, structural components, hormones, and antibodies.
  • Nucleic acids: DNA and RNA — store and transmit genetic information.
🔑 Enzyme: a biological catalyst (usually a protein) that speeds up a specific chemical reaction without being used up itself.

DNA Structure & Function

  • DNA is a double helix made of nucleotides (sugar, phosphate, and a nitrogenous base: A, T, C, G).
  • Base pairing rule: Adenine pairs with Thymine; Cytosine pairs with Guanine.
  • DNA replication is semi-conservative — each new DNA molecule contains one original and one new strand.

Cell Structure

  • Cell membrane: selectively permeable, controls what enters and leaves the cell.
  • Nucleus: contains DNA and controls cell activities.
  • Mitochondria: site of aerobic respiration, produces ATP (the "powerhouse of the cell").
  • Chloroplasts (plant cells only): site of photosynthesis.
  • Plant cells have a cell wall and large central vacuole; animal cells do not.

Mitosis

  • Produces two genetically identical diploid daughter cells from one parent cell.
  • Used for growth, repair, and asexual reproduction.
  • Phases: Prophase (chromosomes condense) → Metaphase (chromosomes align at the equator) → Anaphase (sister chromatids separate) → Telophase (nuclear membranes reform).
🔑 Chromatid: one of two identical copies of a chromosome joined at the centromere, formed after DNA replication.

Meiosis

  • Produces four genetically varied haploid gametes (sex cells) from one parent cell through two divisions (Meiosis I and II).
  • Introduces genetic variation through crossing over (exchange of genetic material between homologous chromosomes) and independent assortment.
  • Essential for sexual reproduction — halves the chromosome number so fertilisation restores the full diploid number.
🔑 Diploid vs Haploid: diploid cells have two sets of chromosomes (2n); haploid cells have one set (n).

Photosynthesis

6CO₂ + 6H₂O + light energy → C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂
  • Occurs in the chloroplasts, using chlorophyll to capture light energy.
  • Light reactions: occur in the thylakoid membranes, split water and produce ATP and oxygen.
  • Dark reactions (Calvin cycle): occur in the stroma, use ATP to fix CO₂ into glucose.

Transport Tissues

  • Xylem: transports water and dissolved minerals upward from roots to leaves (one-way, dead cells).
  • Phloem: transports dissolved sugars (sucrose) from source to sink, in any direction (living cells).
🔑 Transpiration: the loss of water vapour from a plant's leaves, mainly through the stomata, which pulls water up the xylem.

Gas Exchange in Plants

  • Gas exchange occurs mainly through the stomata on leaves, controlled by guard cells that open and close in response to light and water availability.

The Human Circulatory System

  • Arteries: carry blood away from the heart, thick muscular walls, high pressure.
  • Veins: carry blood toward the heart, thinner walls, contain valves to prevent backflow.
  • Capillaries: tiny vessels with walls one cell thick, allowing exchange of gases and nutrients with body tissues.
  • Humans have a double circulatory system: pulmonary circulation (heart ↔ lungs) and systemic circulation (heart ↔ body).
🔑 Double circulation: blood passes through the heart twice on each complete circuit of the body — once for the lungs, once for the body.

Gas Exchange in Humans

  • Gas exchange occurs in the alveoli of the lungs — thin-walled, moist, and richly supplied with capillaries to maximise diffusion.
  • Oxygen diffuses into the blood; carbon dioxide diffuses out, driven by concentration gradients.

Cellular Respiration

C₆H₁₂O₆ + 6O₂ → 6CO₂ + 6H₂O + energy (ATP)
  • Aerobic respiration: requires oxygen, occurs in the mitochondria, releases large amounts of ATP.
  • Anaerobic respiration: occurs without oxygen, produces less ATP and lactic acid (in animals) or alcohol and CO₂ (in yeast/plants).

Levels of Organisation

Biosphere → Biome → Ecosystem → Community → Population → Organism — from the largest scale to the smallest.

🔑 Ecosystem: a community of living organisms interacting with each other and their non-living (abiotic) environment.

Energy Flow & Nutrient Cycling

  • Producers (plants) convert sunlight into chemical energy; consumers obtain energy by eating other organisms; decomposers break down dead organic matter.
  • Energy flows one-way through a food chain/web, decreasing at each trophic level (only ~10% is passed on).
  • Nutrient cycles (e.g. carbon cycle, nitrogen cycle) recycle matter through ecosystems.

Human Impact on the Environment

  • Pollution: air, water, and soil pollution from industry, agriculture, and waste harm ecosystems and human health.
  • Habitat destruction: deforestation and urbanisation reduce biodiversity and displace species.
  • Global warming/climate change: driven by excess greenhouse gases (mainly CO₂), leading to rising temperatures and changing weather patterns.
  • Conservation strategies: protected areas, sustainable resource use, and pollution control help protect biodiversity.

Exam Paper Guide

Understand exactly what the Grade 11 Life Sciences paper looks like and how to maximise your marks.

📄 Paper Overview

  • Duration: 2.5 hours
  • Total marks: 150
  • Section A: Compulsory short-answer/multiple-choice questions
  • Section B & C: Longer structured questions with diagrams, data, and short-answer paragraphs across all topics

🔑 Command Words

  • Name/State/List: 1 mark per correct fact — no explanation needed.
  • Describe: give the characteristics or steps of a process, in order.
  • Explain: give reasons — the biological "why" behind a fact or process.
  • Compare: give both a similarity and a difference between two structures/processes.

Mark Allocation Guide

Question typeMarks per pointWhat's expected
Name/State/List1 markA single correct biological term or fact.
Describe a process1 mark per stepEach correct stage of a process (e.g. mitosis phase) in the right order.
Explain2 marksA fact plus the biological reasoning behind it.
Data-handling/graph questionVariesCorrect reading of data plus a biologically sound interpretation.
📌 Exam tip: Use precise biological terms — "diffusion," "semi-permeable," "chlorophyll" — vague everyday language like "stuff moves through" earns no marks.

Diagram & Process-Writing Guide

Life Sciences exams reward clear diagrams and well-structured process explanations — here's how to do both for full marks.

1

Drawing Biological Diagrams

  • Use a sharp pencil, single continuous lines — never sketchy or shaded lines.
  • Draw large enough to show detail clearly (at least half a page for cell/organ diagrams).
  • Label lines must be straight, drawn with a ruler, and must not cross each other.
  • Always include a title and, where relevant, the magnification or scale.
2

Answering Process Questions (e.g. "Describe mitosis")

Write process answers as a numbered, chronological sequence — examiners award one mark per correct, ordered step.

Step 1: [first stage of the process]
Step 2: [next stage, in the correct order]
Step 3: [continue until the process is complete]
3

Interpreting Graphs & Data

  • Always state the trend first (increases/decreases/stays constant), then quote actual values from the axes as evidence.
  • Link the trend to the underlying biology — don't just describe the shape of the graph.
As [independent variable] increases, [dependent variable] [increases/decreases] from [value] to [value]. This is because [biological reason].
4

Investigations & Variables

  • Independent variable: the factor the experimenter deliberately changes.
  • Dependent variable: the factor that is measured/observed as a result.
  • Controlled variables: factors kept constant to ensure a fair test.
  • Always include a control group/setup to compare results against.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

❌ Messy or Unlabelled Diagrams

Diagrams without a title, labels, or with crossed label lines lose easy marks even if the drawing itself is accurate.

❌ Vague Language

"The cell does its job" isn't an answer — use precise biological terminology every time.

❌ Skipping Steps in a Process

Leaving out one stage of mitosis, meiosis, or photosynthesis costs a mark per missing step.

❌ Describing a Graph Without Explaining It

Stating "the line goes up" without linking it to the underlying biology only earns partial marks.

🔬 Practice tip: Redraw and label the key diagrams (cell structure, mitosis stages, the human heart, a leaf cross-section) from memory each week until you can do it without checking a textbook.
