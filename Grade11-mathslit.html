Study Notes

Interest

  • Simple interest: calculated only on the original amount (principal) every period.
  • Compound interest: calculated on the principal PLUS previously earned interest — grows faster over time.
Simple interest: A = P(1 + i·n)
Compound interest: A = P(1 + i)ⁿ
🔑 P = principal (starting amount), i = interest rate (as a decimal), n = number of time periods, A = final amount.

Banking & Loans

  • Bank accounts (savings, cheque/current) differ in interest earned, fees charged, and access to funds.
  • Loan/hire-purchase repayments involve interest charged on the outstanding balance — understanding a repayment schedule shows how the balance decreases over time.
  • Reading and interpreting a bank statement: opening/closing balance, deposits, withdrawals, and fees.

Inflation, Exchange Rates & Tax

  • Inflation: the general increase in prices over time, reducing the buying power of money.
  • Exchange rates: used to convert one currency into another when travelling or importing/exporting.
  • VAT: Value Added Tax, currently 15% in South Africa, added to the price of most goods and services.
  • Income tax: calculated using tax tables/brackets based on annual income — higher income portions are taxed at higher rates.

Unit Conversions

  • Length: 1 m = 100 cm = 1,000 mm; 1 km = 1,000 m.
  • Volume/Capacity: 1 litre = 1,000 ml; 1 m³ = 1,000 litres.
  • Mass: 1 kg = 1,000 g; 1 tonne = 1,000 kg.

Perimeter, Area & Volume

Rectangle: Area = length × width; Perimeter = 2(l + w)
Triangle: Area = ½ × base × height
Circle: Area = πr²; Circumference = 2πr
Rectangular prism: Volume = length × width × height

Time & Temperature

  • Converting between 12-hour and 24-hour time formats.
  • Calculating time differences, including across time zones.
  • Reading and interpreting temperatures in °C, including negative values.
🔑 Scale drawing: a drawing where all real-world measurements are reduced (or enlarged) by the same ratio, e.g. a floor plan.

Scale

  • Scale is written as a ratio, e.g. 1:50 means 1 unit on the plan represents 50 of the same units in real life.
  • Used on floor plans, maps, and models to represent real-world distances at a manageable size.
Actual length = Length on plan × Scale factor

Reading Maps & Plans

  • Floor plans show room layout, dimensions, and often furniture placement — used to calculate area for flooring, paint, or tiling.
  • Maps use compass directions, distance scales, and legends/keys to represent real locations.
  • Route directions combine distance, direction, and estimated travel time based on average speed.
🔑 Elevation plan: a scale drawing showing a structure from the side, used to represent height and vertical features.

Measures of Central Tendency

Mean = Sum of all values ÷ Number of values
Median = Middle value when data is ordered
Mode = Most frequently occurring value

Measures of Spread

  • Range: the difference between the highest and lowest values in a data set.

Representing Data

  • Bar graphs: compare quantities across different categories.
  • Pie charts: show data as proportions/percentages of a whole.
  • Line graphs: show how a value changes over time.
  • Histograms: show the frequency of continuous, grouped numerical data.
🔑 Bias: when data collection methods (e.g. a poorly chosen sample) unfairly skew results away from representing the true population.

Basic Probability

Probability of an event = Number of favourable outcomes ÷ Total possible outcomes
  • Probability is always a value between 0 (impossible) and 1 (certain), often expressed as a percentage or fraction.
  • Relative frequency: the probability of an event based on observed results from repeated trials, rather than theory.
🔑 Sample space: the set of all possible outcomes of an experiment or event.

Everyday Applications

  • Probability concepts appear in real contexts: weather forecasts (e.g. "60% chance of rain"), games of chance, and risk assessment.
  • Two-way tables and tally tables are used to organise data before calculating probabilities.

Exam Paper Guide

Understand exactly what the Grade 11 Maths Literacy papers look like and how to maximise your marks.

📄 Paper Overview

  • Paper 1: 1.5 hours, 75 marks — basic skills topics (finance, measurement, maps/plans).
  • Paper 2: 1.5 hours, 75 marks — applications topics (data handling, probability), integrated with finance/measurement contexts.

🔑 Levels of Questions

  • Level 1 (Knowledge): straightforward calculations using a given formula.
  • Level 2 (Routine procedures): multi-step calculations in a familiar context.
  • Level 3 (Complex procedures): problems requiring you to decide which method/formula to use.
  • Level 4 (Problem solving): unfamiliar, real-life scenarios requiring interpretation and justification.

Mark Allocation Guide

Question typeWhat earns marks
Direct calculationCorrect formula, correct substitution, correct final answer with units.
Multi-step problemMethod marks awarded at each step, even if the final answer is wrong.
Interpretation questionA correct reading of data/graph/table PLUS a sound conclusion in context.
📌 Exam tip: Always round money to 2 decimal places and include the correct unit (R, %, cm, km) — a correct number without units often loses a mark.

Formula & Calculation Guide

Every key Grade 11 Maths Literacy formula in one place, with a worked example for each.

1

Simple Interest

A = P(1 + i·n)

Example: R5,000 invested at 8% simple interest for 3 years.
A = 5,000 × (1 + 0.08 × 3) = 5,000 × 1.24 = R6,200
2

Compound Interest

A = P(1 + i)ⁿ

Example: R5,000 invested at 8% compound interest for 3 years.
A = 5,000 × (1.08)³ = 5,000 × 1.259712 = R6,298.56
3

Area & Perimeter of a Rectangle

Area = length × width
Perimeter = 2(length + width)

Example: A room is 4 m long and 3 m wide.
Area = 4 × 3 = 12 m²
Perimeter = 2(4 + 3) = 14 m
4

Scale Calculation

Actual length = Plan length × Scale factor

Example: On a 1:100 scale plan, a wall measures 6 cm.
Actual length = 6 × 100 = 600 cm = 6 m
5

Mean, Median & Range

Mean = Sum of values ÷ Number of values

Example: Data set: 4, 7, 7, 9, 13.
Mean = (4+7+7+9+13) ÷ 5 = 40 ÷ 5 = 8
Median (middle value) = 7
Range = 13 − 4 = 9
6

Basic Probability

P(event) = Favourable outcomes ÷ Total outcomes

Example: A bag has 3 red and 7 blue balls. P(red)?
P(red) = 3 ÷ 10 = 0.3 or 30%
